# The Puppet Theatre of Mangiafuoco

> [!info] At a Glance
> A grand 19th-century puppet theatre on a cobbled road, alone. The gilded facade has not been touched in years. Fire damage is visible at one corner if you know to look. The main doors are standing open. Nobody comes here anymore except Mangiafuoco, who never left.

## Overview

The theatre was built over thirty years by one man who believed that puppetry was the highest art and that he would prove it. He was probably right about the first part. The building reflects this ambition without irony: the facade runs sixty feet along the road, gilded plaster columns flanking a triumphal arch, painted murals above the entrance depicting scenes from the great puppet plays, Mangiafuoco's own face ten feet tall on every poster. It was magnificent when it was full. It is still magnificent. That is somehow the worst thing about it — the scale of what it represents, the totality of what was lost, is visible in the architecture whether anyone is inside or not.

The fire was six weeks ago and was contained to the workshop and the bar. The rest of the building — the entrance hall, backstage, stage, and office — was not touched by flame. It was touched by everything else: by the absence of the children who lived here, by the weeks of silence since, by Mangiafuoco's grief hardening into obsession in the one room above the stage. The theatre smells of old smoke and older velvet. The puppets that move through its rooms were built to charm from a distance and are wrong at close range. They are not malicious. They are broken things doing what they were made to do.

This is a great place in slow decline, not a disaster site. Let that land before the players go inside. They need to carry it with them when they finally face the man who built it.

## Exterior

The road delivers you to it without warning — a gap in the treeline, and then suddenly there it is. The scale is the first thing. The second thing is the poster: Mangiafuoco's face, ten feet tall, grinning with a torch in each hand, repeated across the entire facade. Most of the posters are only old and sun-bleached. Along the right side of the building, where the workshop windows are, a few show water-stained and smoke-edged corners. That is the only visible evidence of the fire from out here.

The main doors are partially open. Not forced — simply left that way, as if whoever last went through them did not expect to need them again.

**Perception and Investigation checks — exterior:**

- **DC 10 Perception:** Smoke damage is visible from the workshop windows on the right side of the building, high up — old blackening, weeks at minimum. The stone around those windows is stained with water runoff from the brigade.
- **DC 12 Investigation:** One of the posters, half-torn, shows a list of *Featured Performers*. Lampwick's name appears, with a small engraved portrait. He is smiling. Players who have Roo's pamphlet from Chapter 2 recognise it immediately.
- **DC 10 Perception (passive):** Inside the open doors, somewhere in the dark of the entrance hall, something moves. Slow. Mechanical. A painted smile in the shadow.

> [!tip] GM Note — First Impression
> The exterior does not look like a disaster site. It looks like a theatre that has been closed for a long time and not quite cared for. Play it as beautiful and abandoned, not ruined. Mangiafuoco built this over decades. Let that land before the players go inside.

## The Rooms

> [!tip] GM Note — Orientation and Keys
> Six rooms in total. The **Entrance Hall / Box Office** is always accessible. The **Bar** is accessible through an unlocked door. **Backstage** is locked — requires the Brass Key from the box office booth. The **Puppet Workshop** door is found inside Backstage and is also locked — requires the Iron Key from the same booth. Once inside the Workshop, the players can unbar the door to the **Stage** from within. The **Office** is only reachable after the boss fight, via the key on Mangiafuoco's person.
>
> In short: **get the keys → bar (optional) → backstage → workshop → stage → office.**

### 1. Entrance Hall / Box Office

The foyer hits you with height before anything else — the ceiling is thirty feet up, a faded sky-mural in cracked plaster held up by grand white pillars that rise through the room and disappear into the dark above. The floor is deep red carpet, worn pale down the centre and faintly stained where years have worked at it. At the far end of the hall, closed double doors lead into the theatre seating, a staircase rising on either side toward the balcony. It is the architecture of a place that expected to be full. If the carpet were clean and there were people here, you would think a show was about to start. The smell is old velvet and dust and something faintly floral that has not quite gone, layered under a trace of smoke drifting through from somewhere further in.

**Key Features**

- The box office booth is set into the right-hand wall — dark wood frame, a sliding glass window gone cloudy with years of fingerprints. Through the glass: a brass key and an iron key on hooks on the back wall. And in the corner of the booth, back to the window, arms at its sides, perfectly still: a small painted puppet. It is visible through the dirty glass the moment anyone looks at the booth. Getting to the keys is the room's first puzzle.
- Three Chorus Puppets walk a slow oval at the far end of the hall, near the closed double doors and the staircases. They are not yet aware of the players.
- The puppet in the booth has not moved in some time. Its stillness is deliberate and slightly wrong — the more clearly the players can see it through the glass, the worse it gets.
- The chandelier will shed plaster dust and sway if the floorboards shake hard enough — combat, forcing the booth door. No structural risk, but it provides cover for a Stealth re-roll if used creatively.

**Condition:** Intact but neglected. Dusty. Cobwebs in the upper corners. Faint smoke smell drifting through. Nothing broken, nothing burned.

> [!warning] Encounter — Entrance Hall
> Three Chorus Puppets on their oval loop. **DC 12 Stealth (group check)** to move through the hall without triggering them. Once triggered, they converge. The motionless corner puppet activates at the DM's discretion — when the window breaks, when combat begins, when someone touches it, or never.

> [!tip] GM Note — The Box Office Puzzle
> The keys are visible. Getting to them is the puzzle. No single correct solution — present the booth as a problem and let the players think. Breaking the window: DC 12 DEX, on a fail by 5+ they cut themselves (1d4 piercing) and the noise carries. Forcing the door: DC 14 STR or DC 13 DEX (thieves' tools), both make noise. Looking carefully at the exterior of the booth reveals a coin-return slot at floor level — no check to spot it if they look; DC 11 Sleight of Hand to fish the keys through. Anything sensible should work. The booth is wooden and old. Let them think.

### 2. The Bar

The theatre's refreshment room — a long space with a mahogany bar running the full length of one wall, the shelves behind it still stocked with bottles that have gone to sediment. You can see where the fire came through the workshop wall: the plaster on that side has fallen in grey sheets, the laths beneath exposed and scorched, and in the far corner a section of ceiling hangs at an angle, held by nothing visible, a strip of ornate ceiling-rose the only thing still attached. The floor is warped badly along its whole length, the boards lifted in ridges, the varnish bubbled and gone. It smells of wet char and something sweet — the bottles that exploded in the heat.

**Key Features**

- The collapsed ceiling section at the far end is genuinely structural — if anything slams into the support beam (a Dancer Puppet, a player, a creature knocked prone), make a DC 12 CON save for the structure: on a failure the section comes down, DC 13 DEX save or 2d6 bludgeoning and Restrained under debris.
- Warped floorboards throughout — difficult terrain across the whole room.
- The bar itself provides three-quarter cover for crouched players; the mahogany is solid enough to hold.
- Behind the bar: a jacket on a hook, a mug with a name on it (*CARLO*), a folded newspaper from three years ago. The bar had regulars. It had a life.

**Condition:** Fire-damaged. The workshop-side wall is charred black and partially open to weather. The far corner has partially collapsed. The structural damage makes the room feel genuinely dangerous. *There is nothing essential here* — players who search find only vinegary spirit, old drink receipts, and those personal effects.

> [!warning] Encounter — The Bar
> Two Dancer Puppets in wide, looping arcs — ducking under the hanging ceiling section, curving around overturned tables, appearing and disappearing through smoke-stained air. The space rewards positioning. The bar for cover. The floorboards as difficult terrain throughout.

### 3. Backstage

A vast working space — the engine of a theatre in operation, now at rest. Rope and rigging hang from a ceiling too high to see clearly in the dark above. Painted scenery flats lean against every wall: forests, palaces, oceans, a village in winter, a night sky dense with carefully rendered stars. The craftsmanship is extraordinary. Standing in front of the largest flat — a painted autumn forest — is almost like standing in a real one. The smell here is canvas and sawdust and size, and only very faintly, as an undertone, the ghost of smoke from the rooms beyond. Cobwebs in the rigging. A broken lantern on the floor near the door. Everything still.

**Key Features**

- At the far end, a heavy door in the workshop wall with a keyhole. As the players move toward it, a blue glow becomes visible — not a gas lamp, but a butterfly, made of soft light the colour of a clear sky at dusk, resting on the door handle. Its wings move slightly, as though breathing. If a player moves to touch the handle, it dissolves into the air. No sound, no trace, just gone.
- Four Chorus Puppets cling to the rigging above the midpoint of the space — DC 15 Perception to spot them before they drop, DC 10 if anyone is looking upward.
- The rigging is a resource: grab a hanging rope and swing past the ambush (DC 12 Athletics), or cut a rope to drop rigging on a Puppet (2d6 bludgeoning, DC 11 DEX save).
- Rolled carefully against the far wall: a full set of backdrops for a production of *The Blue Fairy* — clouds and moonlight and a cottage, and at the centre of the largest drop, painted in brilliant blue, a butterfly. Enormous. Painted from life, almost certainly.

**Condition:** Intact but neglected. Dusty, undisturbed, structurally sound. The smoke smell is faint — just a trace in the old canvas.

> [!tip] GM Note — The Blue Butterfly
> She cannot open the door or free Kanga herself. But she can point. Play the butterfly without explanation — do not name it, do not comment on it beyond what the players see. Players who later encounter the Blue Fairy in her other forms may remember this moment differently in hindsight.

> [!warning] Encounter — Backstage Ambush
> Four Chorus Puppets dropping from the rigging when the players reach the midpoint. Swarm Frenzy can activate easily if the players cluster in the narrow spaces between the scenery flats. The trapdoor levers for the stage are mounted near the stage-side wall — DC 10 Investigation to learn which hatch each lever controls. This is relevant in the boss fight.

### 4. The Puppet Workshop

The door opens onto the worst of it — you can see exactly where the fire started. The right half of the room, from the threshold forward, is charred down to bare iron legs. The reference drawings that covered the board on the right wall are gone, the board itself blackened, the wall behind it open to the weather where the fire burned through the plaster entirely. Puppet parts in barrels have melted into each other, fused in postures no puppet would choose. The floorboards on the right side are soft and dark and should not be trusted. And then, past the midpoint of the room, the fire stopped. The left half is intact — the smell of varnish still here, mixed with ash from the other side. Bunkbeds in two rows, small and close together, small shoes at the foot of each one. A list of names on the far wall, written in careful capitals by someone who wanted each one to be correct and legible.

**Key Features**

- Kanga is chained by the ankle to the last workbench on the intact side. Bolt cutters hang on the south wall — just beyond the reach of her chain. She has been looking at them for weeks.
- The soft floorboards on the fire-damaged right side may give way under running weight — DC 12 DEX save, fall through on failure for 1d6 damage and Prone.
- Two Soldier Puppets stand motionless at the far end of the burned half, facing the charred wall. They do not turn until the players are halfway across the room.
- In one of the middle bunkbeds, tucked under the thin pillow: a wooden boy's jacket, folded. Small. Painted wood buttons. DC 11 Perception to find it.

**Condition:** Split. The right half is fire-damaged — dangerous floor, charred structure, open to the elements in one corner. The left half is intact but speaks a different kind of damage: the absence of the children who slept here.

> [!warning] Encounter — The Puppet Workshop
> Two Soldier Puppets activating mid-room, in a space with dangerous terrain on the side that leads to them. Kanga cannot be harmed by the Soldiers — they move around her as though she is part of the room. Once she trusts the players, she will call out useful information: which Soldier is slower, where the floor is safe, the location of the bolt cutters.

> [!tip] GM Note — The Sleeping Side
> After the fight, give the players time here. No enemies. The shoes are the thing — lined up, all different sizes, someone lined them up. A child who takes off their shoes before bed in a strange place has started to think of it as home. Kanga made it bearable enough for that to happen. Don't say this. Let the shoes say it.

### 5. The Stage

Vast. Beautiful even now. The stage is polished wood — scratched and dusty but intact, the boards still true — and it runs the full width of the building, sixty feet from wing to wing. The footlights flicker along the stage's front edge, gas jets, not entirely empty, casting the space in amber and long shadow. Beyond them the auditorium opens out: rows of red velvet seats receding into the dark, the gilt on the balcony rails visible even from here. The ceiling above the stage is hung with scenery that was never taken down — painted clouds, a forest canopy, a section of night sky — swaying slowly on frayed cables whenever there is a draught. The acoustics are perfect. Every footstep echoes. Every voice carries.

Mangiafuoco is at the back of the stage. His back is to the door. He is seated at a small table, running a puppet through a short sequence of movements, again and again. The puppet's left arm is not working correctly. He runs the sequence. The arm slips. He resets, patiently, and runs it again. He does not react to the sound of the door.

**Key Features**

- Multiple trapdoors set into the stage floor — Mangiafuoco knows every one. Players who studied the lever board in Backstage can use a bonus action (if adjacent to the secondary board at the stage edge) to operate one: if Mangiafuoco is using it at that moment, his movement is disrupted.
- The swaying scenery above — frayed cables, damaged painted drops. Can be used as terrain or cover if players think to interact with the rigging.
- Mangiafuoco will only turn and register the players when they mention: the Coachman (or *him*, *he*, *the man*), the children, Pinocchio, or Kanga. When one of these words is spoken, he stops. Sets down the puppet. Turns.
- After the fight, on his person: a large iron key on a chain around his neck. The key to his office. It is cold despite the fire.

**Condition:** Intact. The stage itself was untouched by fire. The auditorium shows only the neglect of weeks — dust on the seats, cobwebs in the upper balcony. The footlights still work. The acoustics still work. It is the most functional room in the building.

> [!warning] Boss Encounter — Mangiafuoco (CR 5)
> See [[Chapter 3 - The Puppet Theatre#5. The Stage — Boss Arena]] for the full stat block, phase two, and trapdoor mechanics. He never says the Coachman's name — only *"him"* or *"he"* or *"the man."* DC 15 Insight: saying the name would make the loss real in a way he cannot survive.

> [!tip] GM Note — The Opening Scene
> Give this moment room to breathe. Mangiafuoco at the table, working the puppet's arm, not turning. The players can stand and listen for a full round. He is somewhere else entirely. The tragic thing is not that he has broken — it is that you can still see the brilliant man underneath, doing exactly what he has always done, in a theatre full of still puppets and old smoke and nobody left. Let the players carry that into the fight.

### 6. Mangiafuoco's Office

The fire did not reach here. The neglect did not quite reach here either. A beautiful room: leather-topped desk, floor-to-ceiling bookshelves dense with reference books and playbills, framed production posters on every wall in close rows, a Persian rug undisturbed, a small brazier cold in the corner. The desk is clear except for a single journal. It is the one room in the theatre that looks as it did before everything happened — and that makes it, in its way, the strangest room of all.

The wall opposite the door is covered entirely in a conspiracy board. Red string. Pinned papers. Circled symbols. It takes up the full wall. He has been doing this since the night of the fire and has not left to act on any of it.

**Key Features**

- The conspiracy board — see [[#The Conspiracy Board]] below.
- The desk journal: entries become more erratic toward the end, handwriting loosening, margins filling with diagrams, dates stopping. The final entry reads: *"The boy Pinocchio — I have never seen anything like it. He breathes. He thinks. He feels. He flinches when he is frightened, not because he was built to but because he is. I only need to understand how."* Below it: a sketch of a jointed wooden arm. The proportions are exact.
- A glass case on a shelf behind the desk, locked. Inside: a puppet. Technically perfect — invisible joinery, extraordinary carving, the finest painted face in the building. It has never moved. DC 12 Investigation: the eyes are not glass as they appear. They are wood, painted over to look like glass. He tried to make them real and could not, and concealed the failure so carefully it almost does not show.
- Books and playbills on every shelf — records of thirty years' work. Players who spend time here will understand what was built and what was taken.

**Condition:** Intact. Deliberately maintained. The one room he could not let go to ruin.

## The Conspiracy Board

*A player-facing description — read or paraphrase to the table.*

On the left side of the board, near the top, a circus poster — bright colours, professional printing, recently made. A travelling show with a long list of acts. In the centre of the illustration: a large tent seen from above in a bird's-eye view. From above, the tent shape is exactly that of a wheel, the central pole like a hub, the guy-ropes like spokes radiating outward. Someone has circled it in red. There is a question mark beside it.

On the right side of the board, level with the poster, a row of missing children notices from a town called *Molino Verde* — Green Mill, several days east. Each notice has been printed with a small engraving of the village to identify it. In the background of every image, behind the rooftops: a water mill on the river. The water wheel in each image has been circled in red.

At the bottom of the board, a shipping company advertisement — *Grand Maritime Freight, established 1842*. Illustrated with crates on a dock, roped together with a decorative knot. One crate bears a pattern in the rope where it crosses: eight fibres radiating outward from a central point. It looks exactly like a ship's wheel. It has been circled.

Three clusters. Three wheels. Red string runs from each of them inward, converging at the centre of the board. At the centre, in large, frantic handwriting — *make HIM pay* — underlined twice, hard enough to tear the paper slightly. And pinned at the absolute centre, where all three strings meet: a plain white business card. No company name. No address. No printing except one image: a black wagon wheel, clean and precise on white card.

He circled the same symbol three times in three different contexts and never left this building. The work is here, complete, waiting for someone with the capacity to use it.

Dark fairytale portrait, gothic storybook style, warm light from the fire behind, cold light on his face.

## Notes for the DM

**The silence mechanic.** This theatre has perfect acoustics — the stage engineers it, and the empty building amplifies it. Players rolling Stealth should be reminded that their footsteps carry. Sneezing, dropping equipment, forcing a door, raising a voice — any significant noise may cause a dormant puppet to activate earlier than anticipated. This rewards planning and punishes carelessness without requiring a die roll for every footstep. It is an atmosphere note as much as a mechanical one.

**The blue butterfly.** It appears at the workshop door in Backstage and should be played without explanation or comment beyond what the players observe. Do not name it. Do not have an NPC react to it. Players who later encounter the Blue Fairy in her other forms will remember this moment differently — not as a mystery, but as a pattern they were part of before they understood it. The meaning accrues backward.

**Mangiafuoco on the stage.** When the players first enter through the workshop door, Mangiafuoco is at the far end with his back to them, running a puppet sequence. Give this scene room. He is somewhere else entirely — not performing, not threatening, just a brilliant man doing the only thing he knows how to do, in a theatre that has gone quiet around him. The players can stand and listen for a full round before he notices them. Let them. What they carry into the fight matters. He is not a monster. He is grief that has had six weeks to calcify into something dangerous.
