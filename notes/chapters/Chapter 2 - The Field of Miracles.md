# What Actually Happened

> [!note] What Actually Happened
>
> **1. P leaves the Inn scared and confused — still convinced the innkeeper was wrong**
>
> I ran east because it was the only direction I had. My heart was hammering and my legs were already going and I did not look back. The innkeeper was angry — I had heard that — but angry did not mean right. The Fox had said he paid. The Fox had said it clearly. I had been sitting right there, full of good bread and warm with it, and the Fox had risen and stretched and announced that everything was taken care of. He would not have lied about that. People did not lie about things like that. I held to this as I ran, the way you hold to something in a high wind, and I did not let go.
>
> ---
>
> **2. P follows the Fox's directions east — the road grows quiet**
>
> The Fox had told me: east past the gate, down the old road toward the hills, past the fork where the hawthorn grows sideways in the ditch. I found the gate. I found the road. I found the hawthorn. The cobblestones gave way to packed earth and then to something softer, darker, and the town fell behind me and there was only the sound of birds and the wind moving in the high trees. It was peaceful, I suppose, if you were not frightened. I was frightened. I walked anyway, because the Field was real and the Field would make everything right.
>
> ---
>
> **3. The giant snake**
>
> I heard it before I saw it — a slow, crushing sound, as if the whole forest were being rearranged, and then nothing, and then the sound again, closer. I stopped. I should not have stopped. Something moved in the undergrowth to my left, something vast and unhurried, and then I saw the head: enormous, flat, the colour of dead leaves, a tongue that tested the air and found me there. It was bigger than anything I had ever imagined a living thing could be. It was bigger than Geppetto's workshop. It was bigger than the whole of myself.
>
> I ran. Not bravely — I did not think, I simply ran, which is not the same thing. The snake did not hurry; it did not need to. I could hear it coming through the brush in that slow, inexorable way, the undergrowth flattening before it, and I scrambled up the nearest tree with my hands and feet moving on their own, faster than I knew I could go, and I wedged myself into the fork of a branch high up and pressed myself against the bark and did not breathe. Below me, the snake moved through the space where I had been standing and continued on, gradually, into the dark between the trees. It did not look up. I stayed in that tree for a long time before I climbed down.
>
> ---
>
> **4. P reaches the Field of Miracles — and overhears everything**
>
> The clearing was beautiful. I had not expected that. A circle of open ground with the forest pressing close on all sides, the light golden and thick the way it gets in late afternoon, the earth dark and soft. Nothing grew in the middle of the clearing. That should have told me something. I dug my little hole with my hands, the way the Fox had said, and I counted my coins in, and I said the words he had told me, and I covered the hole over and smoothed the earth and stepped back. Then I sat down to wait.
>
> They did not know I was there. The Fox and the Cat came out of the trees at the far edge of the clearing maybe half an hour later, walking easily, and the Fox was talking — laughing, almost — and I heard my own name, and I went very still. I heard the Fox say *Lampwick.* I heard him say *sold.* I heard something about the Puppet Theatre of Mangiafuoco and a price and the Fox said *worth every coin* and laughed again. The Cat said nothing. I stayed in the shadow of the trees and I listened and I did not move and I heard everything.
>
> ---
>
> **5. P confronts them — the Fox takes his money anyway**
>
> I came out of the trees because I could not stay hidden. I called out to the Fox and my voice was shaking and I did not care. The Fox turned and his face did a complicated thing — surprise, and then something smooth sliding over the top of it — and then he was smiling, warm and sorry, and he was saying of course, of course, he had been meaning to explain, it was not what it sounded like, it was simply that Lampwick had been given a *wonderful opportunity,* truly, the chance of a lifetime, and the Fox had only been the instrument of something good. I said: where is he. The Fox said: I can tell you that. It will cost you those coins you just buried.
>
> I dug them up. I gave them over. I was frightened and furious and I knew — I knew, somewhere underneath everything — that I was being cheated again. But he told me: the Puppet Theatre of Mangiafuoco, a day's walk east. And that was the one thing I needed, and I took it, and I ran.
>
> ---
>
> **6. P runs — east, toward the Theatre**
>
> I don't know if it was true. I don't know if anything the Fox has ever said to me was true. But I know where I am going now, and I know why, and I will find Lampwick and I will bring him home and that is the only thing that matters. I did not look back. I did not count what was left in my pockets. East. A day's walk. The Puppet Theatre of Mangiafuoco. I ran until I could not run anymore, and then I walked, and I did not stop.

# Chapter Design

> [!abstract] GM Overview
> The party travels east with [[Rabbit]] in search of Pinocchio. They are already one step behind. This chapter is about discovering what happened in the wake of someone who passed through before you — and about finding the 100 Acre Wood changed in ways that hurt. The chapter closes on a moment of genuine warmth: Roo home. The next destination is already clear.
>
> Pinocchio is not present in this chapter. His absence is the chapter's spine.

# On the Road

> [!success] Password: miracles
## The Road East

> [!info] Scene: The Road East
> The road out of the east gate starts pleasantly. The cobblestones soften to packed dirt, the fields open on either side, and the air smells of hay and distance. Rabbit walks briskly and talks in his clipped, efficient way about the Wood — what it's like, how it's organised, who lives there. This is the only time in the chapter where things feel right. Use it to build investment.
> 
> **Rabbit's road talk — beats to weave in naturally:**
> - He lives in a commune in hundred acre woods with his friends
> - Everything was great until Eeyore went missing 2 weeks ago. He didn’t say anything and whilst he doesn’t have much, he left everything behind.
> - Rabbit has been out going village to village looking for Eeyore or clues about what happened to him.
> - People don’t seem to keen on helping animals out as they are not very common.
>
> **Environmental shift:** About a mile out, the pleasant road gives way to something quieter. The birds thin. The undergrowth on either side thickens. A crow watches from a branch. This is where the players start finding the first fragments — a scrap of rope, a bent iron bar half-buried in the ditch. Something large came through here. Something was being moved.

## A Disturbance on the Road

> [!info] Scene: The Broken Cages
> A clearing forty feet off the road, visible between the trees. It takes a DC 10 Perception check to notice it — or no check if anyone is actively looking for the source of the rope fragment.

> [!success] Password: camp

> [!warning] What They Find
> ![[The Camp.PNG]]
> An abandoned campsite just off the road, a few days old. It looks like they left in a hurry.
>A fire pit, cold now. Scattered tools: iron hooks, heavy rope, 2 smashed cages only big enough for a child. A large ripped, tattered tent and a huge smashed cage/cart. This was not two men with a snare; this was a team with equipment and a plan. 
>
> Rabbit goes very quiet. He moves to the tattered tent and crouches by one of the cages. There is a piece of pink striped cloth caught in the hinge.

> [!example] What Rabbit Says
> He does not say much. He turns the cloth over in his hands. He sets it down carefully. After a moment: *"That's Piglet's."* And then, very controlled: *"Please help, there must be something that tells us where they went."*

> [!tip] Skill Checks
> - **DC 6 Investigation:** The camp was abandoned quickly but not in panic. Tools left deliberately where they lay, as though weight was a concern. Whatever they were carrying, they left fast and took only what they could carry.
> - **DC 10 Investigation:** The large cage was ripped open from the inside. The damage in the camp is in a straight-ish line from the cage through the camp.
> - **DC 12 Investigation:** One set of tracks leads away into the deeper woods — very large, heavy, moving in slow curves rather than straight lines. Not a mammal's gait.

> [!success] Password: weasel

> [!warning] The Body
> ![[Dead Weasel.PNG]]
> Lying at the edge of the clearing, is a weasel. Dead. He is small and wiry, with the build of someone who did manual labour and didn't ask questions. He wears a heavy work jacket — slightly too big — and a flat cap pushed back on his head, as though he meant to adjust it and never got the chance. His expression is surprised. He did not see it coming.
>
> The cause is not subtle: the long curved mark of constriction across his ribs. The snake's work. His crew did not wait for him.

> [!success] Password: note

> [!tip] Investigation DC 10 — What's on the Body
> ![[Weasel Note.PNG]]
> - **A crumpled work docket,** folded into the breast pocket of his jacket. Stamped in red ink: a black wagon wheel. The manifest reads: *"12 units — assorted woodland, live delivery, Pleasure Island Wharf, paid on receipt."* No names. No signatures. Just cargo.
> - **20 gold** and a **cheap pocket watch,** still ticking. It keeps good time.

> [!tip] GM Note — What This Scene Does
> This is the moment the players understand something serious has happened. Not just to Pinocchio — to a whole community. The Fox and Cat are one part of a larger pattern. Someone sent these weasels. Someone wanted these animals. This is not the chapter to answer that question; it is the chapter to ask it.

> [!note] Loot — The Rock of Returning
> Lying in the grass at the edge of the camp, half-hidden by a boot print, is a stone. Grey. Smooth. The size of a fist. There is no reason to notice it, and yet one player feels drawn to look at it.
>
> **If any player touches it** — picks it up, nudges it with a foot, pokes it with a stick — it is soul-bound. It will reappear in their pocket or pack within the hour no matter where they leave it. It cannot be given away permanently. It cannot be sold. It is just a stone.
>
> ***Rock of Returning*** *— Wondrous Item, Common (soul-bound on touch)*
> This is a stone. It keeps coming back. There is no research to be done. It is, as far as anyone can determine, simply a stone that has decided to stay with you.
>
> **The hook:** If the bound player throws or kicks the stone out of boredom, frustration, or curiosity, it lands somewhere nearby — as stones do. But the direction it rolls, and where it settles, draws the eye naturally toward the treeline where a thin column of smoke is rising. This is not magic. Probably. The DM may use this moment as a quiet nudge if the party has not yet noticed the Serpent's smoke.

## The Serpent

> [!success] Password: serpent

> [!warning] Scene: Something is Burning
> While the party searches the ruined camp — examining the broken cages, the scattered tools, the dead weasel — a thin column of smoke rises from somewhere in the deep woods to the south. Slow and steady, pale against the tree line, like a distant campfire. Or like someone burning evidence.
>
> Rabbit notices it first. He does not say anything immediately. Then: *"That smoke. It was there when I arrived. I thought it was the weasels' fire."*
>
> **DC 10 Perception:** The smoke is moving. Not drifting with the wind — moving, slow and deliberate, through the trees. Something is carrying the source.
>
> **DC 12 Nature or Survival:** The column is too consistent for a campfire. Too contained. It rises from a single point and moves without spreading. Whatever is producing it is alive.

> [!info] Following the Smoke
> If the party investigates, they push into the undergrowth. The trees thicken. The ground is soft and dark, scored with heavy drag marks — something large has been moving through here repeatedly, in slow, winding curves.
>
> The smoke is closer now. They can see where it seems to come from: behind a fallen oak, in a hollow between two roots. From this angle it could be a smouldering log, or the remnant of a campfire, or anything. They are still close enough to the camp that they can hear the creak of the broken cage in the wind.
>
> Then the smoke thickens — suddenly, without warning, as though something has shifted — and the source withdraws. Smoothly. Silently. Pulling back into the shadow of the deep wood. And before anyone can process that, the forest behind them erupts.

> [!warning] Boss Encounter: The Great Serpent
> ![[The Serpent.PNG]]
>
> *An immense Serpent with a bright green skin, fiery eyes which glowed and burned, and a pointed tail that smoked like a chimney. Even from afar one could see the flash of its red eyes and the column of smoke which rose from its long, pointed tail.*
>
> It rears from the undergrowth in a single motion — enormous, slow, completely unhurried. Bright green scales catch what light filters through the canopy. Two eyes, deep red, that seem to glow from within. And at the far end of its body, coiling through the ferns: a long pointed tail from which the smoke still rises, thick now, curling upward into the trees.
>
> The smoke was the only warning. They followed it right to it.
>
> **In the moment before combat:**
> - The Serpent does not strike immediately. It tastes the air. It looks at the party with those burning eyes the way a creature looks at something it has already decided about.
> - Rabbit takes one step back. He does not speak.
> - A slow, rhythmic sound from deep in the forest — something vast readjusting its weight. The scored drag marks in the earth suddenly make a different kind of sense.

> [!info] Stat Block — The Great Serpent
> **CR** 2 | **Type** Beast | **Size** Large | **Alignment** Unaligned
>
> | STR | DEX | CON | INT | WIS | CHA |
> |-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
> | 19 (+4) | 14 (+2) | 12 (+1) | 2 (-4) | 10 (+0) | 3 (-4) |
>
> **HP:** 5d10+5 (avg 32) | **AC:** 12 (natural armour) | **Speed:** 30 ft., swim 30 ft.
> **Skills:** Perception +2, Stealth +4
> **Senses:** Blindsight 10 ft., passive Perception 12
>
> **Special Traits:**
> - *Smoking Tail.* The Serpent's tail continuously emits a thin column of smoke. Creatures within 30 ft. of the tail automatically spot it — no check required. The Serpent cannot suppress this trait.
> - *Frightening Presence.* Any creature that starts its turn within 15 ft. of the Serpent must succeed on a DC 12 Wisdom saving throw or be frightened until the end of its next turn.
>
> **Actions:**
> - *Bite.* +6 to hit, reach 10 ft., one target. Hit: 2d6+4 piercing.
> - *Constrict.* +6 to hit, reach 5 ft., one Large or smaller creature. Hit: 2d6+4 bludgeoning. Target is grappled (escape DC 14) and restrained. The Serpent can constrict one creature at a time.

> [!tip] Terrain — Use the Environment
> The hollow between the roots is the Serpent's refuge — total cover for a Medium creature but the Serpent itself cannot return during combat. Broken cage timbers from the camp are one round away — useful as improvised cover (half cover, AC +2) or weapons (1d4 improvised). Trees can be climbed (DC 11 Athletics) to stay above constrict range. The scored drag marks throughout this area count as difficult terrain.

> [!success] The Clever Solution — Animal Handling
> The Serpent did not choose to be here. It was the weasels' prize catch — caged for transport to Pleasure Island — and it escaped when they fled. It is not evil. It is frightened, territorial, and enormous.
>
> A player who explicitly connects the evidence ("it escaped the cage — it's been trapped here, not hunting") and tries to communicate that understanding may attempt:
>
> - **DC 15 Animal Handling** to begin calming the Serpent. On a success, it stops attacking and retreats.
> - This DC drops to **DC 12** if the player explicitly references the broken cage or the weasels' equipment.
> - The Serpent cannot be fully tamed. It can only be de-escalated.
>
> **Reward for de-escalation:** The Serpent retreats into the deep woods. Coiled in its hollow is a small cache — coins, a torn map fragment, items left behind by the weasels. The party also gains advantage on the first encounter roll of Chapter 3, from the focus that came from solving this without violence.

> [!tip] After the Fight
> The path ahead opens. The trees begin to thin and the light changes — warmer, softer, golden in a way the road has not been. The smell reaches them first: honey, wood smoke, the particular sweetness of autumn leaves. Rabbit straightens up and walks faster.

# The Hundred Acre Wood

> [!info] Scene: Arrival at the Wood
> *The GM should slow down here. Let the description land.*
>
> It comes on suddenly — a shift in the quality of everything. The light turns the particular amber of late afternoon even though it is still midday. The air smells of honey and wood smoke and something almost edible. The trees are older here, enormous and comfortable-looking, their roots curling above the ground like armchairs. A small stream runs over smooth stones, unhurried.
>
> It is impossibly gentle after the road.
>
>The place is empty. Doors ajar, gardens trampled. On the ground bits of rope and the odd tool, the same as the camp you just left.

> [!example] Rabbit's Composure
> Rabbit says nothing for a moment. He stands at the edge of the Wood and breathes.
>
> *"They were all here. I was only gone a few days."*
>
> That is all he says. Then he straightens his coat and walks toward his burrow, and the party follows, because there is nothing else to do.

> [!success] Password: roo

> [!success] Scene: Finding Roo
> A sound cuts through the quiet — a child crying. Following it leads to Kanga's house, small and still amid the trampled gardens. Inside, curled up alone on the floor, is a small kangaroo joey in a blue shirt. He is dirty, tired, and has clearly been here for some time. How long has he been alone?
>
> ![[Roo.PNG]]
>
> Rabbit crouches beside him without a word and stays there until Roo quiets. Then Rabbit looks up at the party over Roo's head.
>
> *"He's all right. Go on ahead to the Field — it's at the eastern edge of the Wood, ten minutes along the track. I'll stay with him."*
>
> Rabbit does not say this casually. He means: someone needs looking after here, and the party is better placed to handle what is waiting at the Field.

> [!info] Key Locations — First Pass
> As the party moves through the Wood toward the Field of Miracles, they pass through or near:
>
> - **Pooh's tree ("MR SANDERS")** — the door is ajar. Inside: honey pots knocked over, one shattered on the floor. Pooh's chair pushed back at an angle. The little fireplace cold. On the table, a smackerel half-eaten.
>
> - **Piglet's house (Trespassers Will)** — The pressed flowers inside are undisturbed. The very small armchair is empty.
>
> - **Rabbit's warren** — organised, tidy. A second pencil still on the desk beside an unfinished list. Rabbit looks at the list and does not pick it up.
>
> - **The Thoughtful Spot** — a log beside a slow stream, worn smooth from use. Pooh's favourite thinking place. Empty. The grass around it is still pressed down from the last time someone sat there.

> [!tip] GM Note — The Base Camp
> The Wood is safe. Completely safe. No hostile encounter can begin here — it is protected by the same gentle magic that makes it what it is. The party can take a Long Rest here freely. They level up here. They return here after every chapter. It is, in every meaningful sense, home.
>
> Frame this to the players directly, out of character if needed: *The Hundred Acre Wood is your base camp. You always come back here. When you bring someone home, the Wood comes back a little more. Right now it is quiet and hurting. That is temporary. You will fix it.*

# The Field of Miracles

> [!success] Password: fox

![[The Field of Miracles.PNG]]

> [!info] Scene: The Clearing
> The Field of Miracles is at the eastern edge of the Wood — not far, a ten-minute walk along a track that gets gradually less tidy. The clearing announces itself. Beautiful, in an unsettling way: the light is too golden, the soil too dark, the silence more complete than even the empty Wood's. Nothing grows in the patches of earth in the centre of the clearing. Nothing has grown there for a long time.

> [!example] What They Find
> [[The Fox]] and [[The Cat]] are here — and they have not noticed the party.
>
> The Cat is digging with patient, methodical attention, turning up the dark earth one handful at a time. This is what they do: someone buries coins here on the Fox's instruction, convinced by a story about gold growing like seeds if you say the right words and believe hard enough. The Fox and Cat return later to collect what was left behind. The Cat digs. The Fox watches.
>
> The Fox stands slightly apart, speaking in a low and unhurried voice — the tone of a man recounting an afternoon that went exactly as planned. He sounds pleased with himself. He does not raise his voice. He never does.

## The Fox and the Cat Encounter

> [!warning] Encounter: The Fox & The Cat
> The Fox and Cat have not noticed the party. They are absorbed in their own afternoon — the Cat digging, the Fox talking. The party has time.

![[The Fox and the Cat.PNG]]

> [!info] Approaching — What the Party Can Observe
> The party can hold at the treeline and watch. From here, they can see clearly: the Cat digging, the Fox standing a little apart with the cane, the Fox talking in a low and unhurried voice.
>
> **DC 10 Perception** (or simply waiting and listening): The Fox's voice carries. He is telling the Cat about selling someone a pair of shoes. This mark hadn't realised the 2 shoes in the box didn't even match.
>
> The Cat says nothing. He keeps digging.
>
> They can observe as long as they like. The Fox and Cat are not going anywhere.

> [!example] The Conversation — Before Combat
> When the party reveals themselves, the Fox turns without apparent surprise. He assesses them in a moment, decides, and is at ease. He has nothing to fear from bounty hunters. He talks for a living, and he is very good at it.
>
> He speaks softly and without hurry. He is in an agreeable mood.
>
> **Key information — what the Fox will share, in his own time:**
>
> - **He never learned either child's name.** Names are a complication he avoids as a matter of habit. He handled a boy recently — that is as specific as he gets by default.
> - **If the party asks about a missing boy without specifying a puppet,** the Fox assumes they mean Lampwick. *"The boy. Yes. That was a tidy piece of work."*
> - **On Lampwick:** *"We took him exactly where he wanted to go. The Theatre. Got a pretty penny for delivering him there as well."* He says this without apology. It was a clean job; there is nothing to dress up.
> - **On Pinocchio:** *"The puppet was bad timing on my part, I'll admit to that. He overheard us chatting — about dropping off the boy, the state of the Theatre. Ran off before we could have a word."* A dry smile. *"Maybe Mangiafuoco will give him a part."*
>
> **Insight checks:**
> - **DC 12 Insight:** The Fox is a conman and a smooth talker but you can tell he is telling the truth here. He is not worried. Not remotely. He does not believe he has done anything wrong. He helped the boy and got paid for it.
> - **DC 14 Insight:** He knew he would get paid for Lampwick. This probably wasn't his first time dealing with the Theatre.
>
> **Combat trigger:** The Fox and Cat will not start a fight. They have coin in their pockets, the afternoon is pleasant, and the Cat is still digging. Combat begins only if:
> - The party attacks, or
> - The party has extracted all of the above and the Fox has grown bored. He can see the party won't just step aside. He will look at the Cat once — briefly — and that will be the signal.

> [!warning] Combat: The Fox and The Cat
> The Fox fights without enthusiasm. He is not here to brawl; he is here to leave. Everything he does is aimed at creating the window.
>
> The Cat fights to give him that window. He is the harder of the two to stop.

> [!example] The Reveal — Opening Beat of Combat
> When the Fox decides things have reached this stage, he does something that lands differently than expected.
>
> He sighs. He adjusts his grip on the cane.
>
> And then, with a sound like a whisper of metal on wood, the silver fox-head handle rises away from the shaft, and in one unhurried motion he draws a longsword — the cane's sheath clattering hollow to the ground at his feet. He holds it loosely, point down, as though this is all very tedious.
>
> *"I do so hate it when things reach this stage."*
>
> **Delivering this moment:** Let the players register it before anything moves. Nobody who saw him standing in the clearing with a walking cane expected a sword. If anyone examined the cane before this — or tried to disarm him — they feel that surprise twice as hard. The Fox does not flourish it. That is the point. A flourish would suggest he finds this exciting. He does not.
>
> **Mechanically:** For this fight, the Fox uses the Veteran's Cane as a longsword (1d8 slashing, one-handed, Sap mastery — see loot below). His *Silver Cane* action in his stat block becomes a longsword attack at +1 to hit, 1d8-1 slashing. He still hates using it. He is still looking for the exit.

> [!info] The Escape — Smoke Bomb
> When the Fox is ready to leave — whether he has taken damage, grown bored, or simply decided the conversation is over — there is no drama. A smoke bomb drops at his feet. Grey, thick, already blooming before anyone can react. He is moving before the smoke reaches knee height. The Cat is two steps behind him.
>
> By the time the air clears, they are gone.
>
> The Fox does not call anything back over his shoulder. He has already said everything he intended to say.

> [!tip] What the Party Learns
> From the conversation — or from Roo, if they missed it — the players come away with:
>
> - **Lampwick was delivered to the Puppet Theatre of Mangiafuoco.** Not taken by force — arranged, paid for, considered by the Fox to have been entirely reasonable.
> - **Pinocchio overheard the Fox and Cat** discussing Lampwick and the Theatre. He ran east, alone. He is already ahead.
> - **The Theatre is a day's walk east.** This is where Chapter 3 begins.

> [!note] Loot — Left Behind in the Retreat
> Two things are left in the clearing when the smoke clears. The Fox did not drop them by accident. He was moving fast and they are heavy and he can always get more.
>
> ***Veteran's Cane*** *— Wondrous Item, Common (The Fox's)*
> A handsome black walking cane with a silver fox-head handle, worn smooth from long use. As a **Bonus Action**, the holder transforms it into an ordinary Longsword, or back into a cane. The holder must be holding it in either case.
> Its real value is social: a creature that does not know the cane's nature will never attempt to disarm you of it. It would be unconscionable to take a walking stick from someone who needs it. The Fox has relied on this assumption for years.
>
> **As a Longsword (weapon stats):**
> - **Damage:** 1d8 slashing (one-handed) | 1d10 slashing (two-handed, Versatile)
> - **Weight:** 3 lb. | **Category:** Martial melee
> - **Mastery:** Sap — on a hit, the target has Disadvantage on its next attack roll before the start of your next turn
> - No magical bonus to attack or damage — the sword is ordinary once drawn
>
> ***One-Way Glasses*** *— Wondrous Item, Common (The Cat's)*
> A pair of round black glasses. While worn, everyone around you assumes you are blind — you move differently, hold yourself differently, and people instinctively give you the benefit of every doubt. You are not blind. You can see through them perfectly well — mostly. You have **disadvantage** on all attack rolls and Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks while wearing them, and must succeed on a **DC 10 Dexterity saving throw** when moving at full speed or stumble.
> The Cat never spoke. It is possible nobody expected him to.

# Wrap-up
## Roo's Return

> [!success] Scene: Return to the Wood
> When the party comes back from the Field, Rabbit is where they left him — with Roo, now settled, sitting on the floor and looking at something small he found in his pocket. He looks up when he hears them coming.
>
> Roo asks the party seventeen questions without pausing for answers. Then he hugs Rabbit's leg.
>
> Rabbit allows himself one moment — brief, visible — of something that is not quite composure. Then he produces a notebook.

> [!info] If the Party Missed Information at the Field
> If the players did not extract everything they needed from the Fox, Roo has it.
>
> He was there. Earlier — before the party arrived — he had followed the sound of voices out of the Wood, the way he follows anything interesting, and then he heard the Fox and Cat talking and went very still in the undergrowth because something about them felt immediately wrong. He stayed hidden and listened until they moved on. Then he went home and cried, because he had been alone a long time and that had frightened him more than anything.
>
> He doesn't know any names — nobody used them — but he tells the party what he heard: a boy was taken to a theatre, they were paid for the delivery, and a puppet overheard the whole thing and ran east to find him.
>
> He says this carefully, the way someone does when they know they are telling you something that matters. Then he shows them what he picked up from the grass near where the Fox had been standing, before he ran.

> [!tip] The Milestone — Level 2
> *Tell this to the players as part of the fiction, not outside it.*
>
> With the party back, the next step clear, and Roo safe beside them, something in the Wood shifts. Small things — the light changes slightly, a bird somewhere in the deep branches tries a tentative note, the stream seems a little louder. Roo finds the Thoughtful Spot, sits on the log, and immediately begins examining a beetle. The Wood has one of its own again, and seems to know it.
>
> *The party feels something settle. A readiness. A sense that they are, collectively, more than they were this morning.*
>
> Tell them to level up. They are Level 2.

> [!tip] GM Note — Future Milestone Beats
> Each Pooh character who returns follows this same structure. Something in the Wood changes — the honey smell intensifies, Piglet's pressed flowers reappear in their frames, Eeyore's corner is damp and familiar again. Describe the Wood coming alive one piece at a time. By the time all characters are home, it should feel full and warm and *right* again — a strong enough place to face whatever the final chapter brings.
