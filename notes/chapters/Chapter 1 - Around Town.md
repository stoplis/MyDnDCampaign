# What Actually Happened

> [!note] What Actually Happened
>
> **1. P leaves home with money for the bank**
>
> That morning, Geppetto pressed a small purse into my hands — the shop's takings, heavy with coins — and sent me on my way to school, with a stop at the bank to deposit them first. The sun was bright and the cobblestones warm, and I swung the purse as I walked, feeling very grown-up and trusted. It was a simple errand. I had done it before.
>
> ---
>
> **2. P drops a coin and is spotted by the Fox and Cat**
>
> Somewhere near the square, a coin slipped free and rang against the street. I scrambled for it, and when I looked up, two pairs of eyes were already watching me — a Fox, sleek and smiling, and a Cat, quiet as a shadow at his side. They had seen the purse. I could tell from the way the Fox's tail flickered that he had already begun to think.
>
> ---
>
> **3. The Fox offers breakfast and speaks of a way to help Geppetto**
>
> He was so kind about it. The Fox crouched to my level and asked my name, and before long he was speaking of poor dear Geppetto — how hard the old man worked, how he deserved better — as if he had known him for years. He said there was a way, a wonderful way, to ease Father's troubles forever. But first, he said, I must be hungry. Let him buy me breakfast. We could talk more at the inn.
>
> ---
>
> **4. P, the Fox, and the Cat go to the Inn**
>
> The Red Crawfish Inn smelled of bread and woodsmoke, and the Fox chose a corner table and ordered everything with a sweep of his paw. The Cat ate without speaking, which I found strange, but the Fox talked enough for all three of us. I sat between them and listened, my purse tucked tight in my lap, trying very hard to be sensible.
>
> ---
>
> **5. The Fox tells P about the Field of Miracles**
>
> He leaned in close then, voice dropping to a whisper, and told me about the Field of Miracles — a place not far from here, he said, where if you buried your coins in the earth and said the right words, they would multiply overnight. A handful of gold becomes a hundred. A hundred becomes a thousand. Enough, he said softly, to set Geppetto free from his workbench forever. My heart was hammering. I believed every word.
>
> ---
>
> **6. The Fox says they have paid and takes his leave**
>
> When the plates were empty the Fox rose, stretched, and said they must be going — but not to worry, he had already settled the bill with the innkeeper, everything was taken care of. He gave me directions to the Field and pressed my hand warmly, and then he and the Cat were gone through the door before I could even thank them properly. I sat for a moment, full and hopeful and not yet afraid.
>
> ---
>
> **7. The innkeeper demands payment — P runs**
>
> The innkeeper appeared at my elbow almost immediately, red-faced and holding out his hand. I told him there must be some mistake — the Fox had said he had already paid, had said so very clearly. But the innkeeper's face only grew darker, and his voice rose, and I said it again, more carefully, that the Fox had settled it, that surely he remembered. He did not remember. He remembered nothing of the sort. The more I insisted, the louder he became, and people were turning to look, and I felt the heat of it — his certainty pressing against mine — and something in me simply gave way. I ran. Not because I believed I had done anything wrong. I ran because I was frightened, and the shouting would not stop, and I did not know what else to do.
>
> ---
>
> **8. P goes to the Field of Miracles**
>
> Even then, even frightened and breathless and confused, I went. The Fox had been so kind, and he had said he had paid, and I believed him. The innkeeper must have made a mistake — these things happened. And the Field of Miracles was still there, still real, and if it worked — when it worked — then there would be coins enough to quiet any dispute. I followed the directions the Fox had given me, out past the edge of town and into the quiet beyond, still certain I had done nothing wrong.

# Chapter Design

> [!abstract] GM Overview
> The players are hired by Geppetto *(jeh-PET-oh)* at the start of this chapter. Pinocchio *(pih-NOH-kee-oh)* disappeared two days ago — heading for the bank, then school — and never came home. The chapter is an investigation: a slow accumulation of small observations that assembles into a complete picture. The picture, when complete, points east to the Red Crawfish Inn. What happens there closes the chapter and opens the road.
>
> The town is small enough to walk in an afternoon. Not every location needs to be visited — three or four will name the Inn. A second thread runs underneath: Lampwick *(LAMP-wik)*, the baker's boy from next door, is also missing. He's not a victim — he went willingly — but the players don't know that yet.

> [!tip] GM Note — How the Clues Chain
>
> The investigation is non-linear. Players can start anywhere and fan out in any order. The clues are distributed but converge naturally on the Inn.
>
> 1. **Workshop → Square:** Geppetto gives Pinocchio's route (bank, then school). The square is the first obvious stop. The bakery is directly next door and rewards players who think to knock before heading out.
> 2. **The Bakery (optional, adjacent):** Immediately next door to the Workshop — easy to miss, rewarding to visit. Emilio's quiet restraint and Carmela's open distress establish that a second child is missing, by a different route and for different reasons. Carmela may press something tangible into the party's hands: a note Lampwick left, or his careful drawing of the Mangiafuoco poster. Crucially: Lampwick left *later* than Pinocchio and was heading north through the square — if the Fox and Cat were already working Pinocchio when he passed through, he may have seen something from a different angle. This is the clearest early thread pointing toward the theatre, separate from and running beneath the Fox and Cat scheme.
> 3. **Square → Bank (can run in parallel):** The square witnesses name the Fox and Cat and confirm the direction: east, not west toward the bank. The bank clerk confirms Pinocchio never arrived and places him on the east side of the square — already heading the wrong way.
> 4. **Cobbler (efficient):** Gives the clearest pointer to the Inn directly. Establishes Fox and Cat as serial criminals with a file, not opportunists.
> 5. **School (optional but resonant):** Deepens the Lampwick thread. Not needed to reach the Inn, but essential for the campaign's larger shape. Serafina Conti's specific concern — "obsessed," the posters, absences that felt like preparation — complements what the Bakery gave the party, and makes Lampwick's intentions harder to dismiss. The Mangiafuoco posters connect both locations.
> 6. **→ The Inn:** Every thread ends here. The Fox and Cat were seen going in. The ruckus happened here. The boy ran east from here. The bill is unpaid.
>
> Players don't need every location — three or four are enough to name the Inn with confidence. The Bakery and the School are the two threads that build the Lampwick arc; the party needs at least one of them for that story to land later. The police officer will be unhelpful wherever he's encountered, but will grudgingly confirm the Fox and Cat are a known problem. The coin in the workshop floor and the Mangiafuoco posters are small rewards for players who look carefully.

# Intro
## Geppetto’s Workshop

> [!info] **Geppetto's Workshop and Home** *(south street)*
> *A narrow street south of the square, cobblestones smooth from decades of traffic, a smell of fresh bread drifting from the bakery next door. Geppetto's workshop occupies the ground floor; the family lives above. The door is unlocked and half-open. Inside: sawdust, linseed oil, the cold smell of an unlit brazier. Half-finished puppets hang from hooks along the ceiling. The stool at Geppetto's workbench is pushed back at an angle, as if someone stood up quickly and didn't think to straighten it.*
>
> ![[Geppetto.png]]
>
> **Key NPC: Geppetto** *(📖 Book / 🎬 Film)* — Small, white-haired, red-eyed from a night without sleep, doing his best not to show how frightened he is. He is proud and does not ask for help easily. When he speaks, he sticks to facts. *He loves Pinocchio completely and without reservation, and this is the one truth in everything he says that doesn't require checking.*
>
> > [!example]- Geppetto's Account
> > Two days ago, Pinocchio left at half past seven. The errand was simple: deposit the week's shop takings at the Savings Bank on the west side of the square, then on to school. The purse held the full week's earnings — roughly eight gold pieces. The boy was in good spirits, swinging the purse as he went. Geppetto watched from the upstairs window until he turned the corner. He did not come home for lunch. He did not come home for supper. The schoolmistress visited after school: Pinocchio was not in class. Geppetto went to check with the bank. The deposit was never made. He filed a report with the town watch the following morning. Nothing has come of it. No investigation. No follow-up. Nothing.
>
> **What players can learn here:**
> - Pinocchio was carrying a significant sum (the week's takings, roughly 8 gold pieces). His errand: bank first, then school.
> - His route: out the door, left onto the south street, right into the square, west to the bank.
> - **The bakery next door:** Run by Emilio *(eh-MEEL-yoh cheh-RAH-toh)* and Carmela Cerato *(kar-MEH-lah cheh-RAH-toh)* — Geppetto's most devoted customers, who have bought his puppets for their son Lampwick for years. Their boy — Lampwick, Pinocchio's closest friend — is also missing. But he left *considerably later* than Pinocchio, was running late, and departed after Pinocchio had already gone.
> - When Geppetto and Emilio went to officer **Leopoldo** *(lay-oh-POL-doh kah-REE-nee)* he told them to come back tomorrow, "boys are always causing trouble and running away, I can't waist my time on nuisance children!". As far as Geppetto can tell nothing has been done.

# Exploring the town

> [!tip] GM Note — The investigation begins
> The players are now free to explore the town as they see fit. They may follow up on leads and hunches.
## The Bakery

> [!info] **The Bakery** *(south street, next door to the Workshop)*
> *The neighbouring shopfront is warm and flour-scented — the windows are steamed, the smell of this morning's bread still hanging in the doorway. A painted sign above the door reads* Il Forno Cerato. *The door is ajar. Inside: round loaves on a rack, sacks of flour against the far wall, a large iron oven that sits cold — nobody lit it properly today. The counter is unattended. Someone has been crying recently.*
>
> ![[The baker.png]]
> The shop owner, **Emilio**, and his wife **Carmela**, have been Geppetto's nearest neighbours for twenty years — and his most devoted customers. Emilio has bought Geppetto's puppets for his son Lampwick since the boy could walk; Lampwick's room is full of them. The two families have grown close the way neighbouring households do, slowly and without quite noticing. Their son Lampwick is Pinocchio's closest friend — and, as of this morning, also missing.
>
> **What players can learn here:**
> - Lampwick left for school *after* Pinocchio — running late, in a hurry, heading north toward the square.
> - Lampwick has been collecting the Mangiafuoco *(man-jah-FWOH-koh)* posters for weeks — pulling them off posts, pinning them up in his room. Several torn ones are still there. His mother can take the players up to see if they ask. The collection makes the obsession unmistakable. Geppetto's puppets are there too, lining the shelves — a dozen of them, each one a gift over the years.
> - His parents last saw him heading north toward the square. He has not come home since.

> [!example] **Emilio** *(baker, Lampwick's father)*
>
> **Personality:** Warm and deliberate, with a craftsman's patience in the way he measures his words. He is frightened and working hard not to show it; the effort is visible. His hands keep finding small tasks — straightening a display, wiping the counter — because stillness means thinking, and thinking is worse right now. He and Geppetto have been neighbours for twenty years; Emilio has bought Geppetto's puppets for Lampwick since the boy could walk. He doesn't say it out loud, but it sits in the room between the two missing boys.
>
> If the players seem trustworthy — a **DC 12 Insight** check to read the room, or simply treating him with patience rather than urgency — he lowers his voice and confides: Lampwick has been obsessing over the Mangiafuoco posters for weeks. Pulling them off posts. Pinning them in his room. Talking about nothing else — the theatre. *"I told him it was foolishness. I should have talked to him properly instead."* He does not say he fears Lampwick left willingly. He doesn't have to say it plainly. The puppets Geppetto made him are still on the shelf. But this morning, his good boots were gone.
>
> **What he knows:**
> - Lampwick left later than Pinocchio — running late, heading north toward the square. Emilio watched him go from the shop doorway.
> - He has been collecting the Mangiafuoco posters for weeks. Not just looking at them — keeping them. Several are pinned in his room upstairs.
> - He noticed the intensity long before this morning and said nothing useful about it. This is what he is actually ashamed of.

> [!example] **Carmela** *(Lampwick's mother)*
>
> **Personality:** Warmer and more openly distressed than her husband. She has been at the window most of the morning — *"a mother knows"* — and she is not pretending otherwise. She does not perform calm. Her hands move constantly; her eyes keep going to the door. She is certain something is wrong, not dramatically but in the quiet, bodily way of someone whose instincts are far ahead of the facts.
>
> She has already decided the players are going to find her son. She treats them accordingly — as people who will need everything useful she can give them.
>
> **What she knows:**
> - Lampwick left after Pinocchio, hurrying, heading north through the square.
> - There are more Mangiafuoco posters in his room — she will take the players up to see, if they ask. The collection makes his intentions hard to dismiss.
> - She does not know exactly what happened to him. But she is certain it involves those posters. *"A mother knows,"* she says, with the flat conviction of someone who has already passed through doubt and come out the other side.

## The Town Square

> [!info] **The Town Square** *(centre)*
> *The square opens up between a cloth merchant's awning and a fish-seller's stall, loud with late-morning trade. Pigeons scatter between the wheels of a slow cart. The cobblestones are close-fitted and smooth; a dropped coin would ring here and everyone within twenty feet would hear it. Around the square's edges, nailed to posts and shuttered shopfronts: colourful printed posters advertising* The Puppet Theatre of Mangiafuoco *— bright reds and golds dates suggesting a new show is coming soon. Professionally printed. Eye-catching. Several have already been torn down by children who wanted to keep them. At the bottom of each poster, in very small print: *"Touring by arrangement of the Grand Coach Company."* Most people don't read the small print.*
> ![[Poster.PNG]]
>
> **Key NPCs — Three Witnesses:**
>
> Each of the following saw something. Separately, each account is partial. Together they build a complete picture: the Fox and Cat approached Pinocchio near the centre of the square, knew his name before he gave it, and walked with him east toward the Inn.
> > [!tip] GM Note — Using the Witnesses
> > The players don't need all three — any two give the essential information. The third adds texture. Give each NPC a distinct manner: Fiamma is forthcoming but requires framing; Nico is wry and transactional (buy a paper first); Delia is helpful but pressed — she has a route to finish, and she'll point you to everyone else. Together they confirm: the Fox and Cat knew Pinocchio by name, they were working him, and they all went east.
>
> > [!tip] GM Note — The Nursery Rhyme
> > Children in the square may be heard singing this rhyme. Adults shush them if they notice. Nobody knows where it came from — it's just always been around.
> >
> > *"When you see the round mark — like a wheel, like a tent —*
> > *Don't ask where the children who followed it went.*
> > *He'll offer you wonders, a ride somewhere far —*
> > *Stay home, little children, stay where you are.*
> >
> > *Round goes the millstone, and round goes the night,*
> > *Round goes the thing at the edge of the light.*
> > *His horses are silent, his coat is the same —*
> > *Sleep fast, little children. The Coachman has come."*
> >
> > The rhyme means nothing to the players yet. Don't draw attention to it. If they ask, any adult says it's just an old song. It's been sung in towns across the region for as long as anyone can remember.
>
> **The Mangiafuoco Posters:** Environmental foreshadowing. They look exciting and are clearly aimed at children. The party may notice that Pinocchio's name appears on one — a testimonial, almost certainly faked. Background detail now; it will matter in a later chapter.
>
> **The Noticeboard:** On the eastern edge of the square, among the usual notices of lost livestock and goods for sale, there is a child's missing notice — faded, three months old, from a village a day's walk east. The name and description have been written in a careful, unpractised hand. Someone has placed a small bunch of dried flowers beneath the post. Nobody has taken the notice down.

### The Flower Seller

> [!example] **The Flower Seller *(Fiamma Ferraro)*
> ![[The Flower Seller.png]]
> - **Fiamma Ferraro, the flower seller** *(FYAH-mah feh-RAH-roh)* — Stout, sharp-eyed, stationed near the south entry of the square. She has everything to tell — but she doesn't want to look like a gossip. She needs to feel like she's serving justice, not satisfying curiosity. She noticed the coin fall. She noticed the two strangers appear. *"The fox was very charming — the sort that makes your purse feel lighter just from standing nearby."* She watched all three walk east together.
>
> > [!tip] GM Note — Fiamma's Social Layer
> > Fiamma holds back initially — not from hostility, but from dignity. She doesn't want to be seen as a busybody. Unlock her full account with one of the following:
> > - **DC 10 Persuasion** — frame it as "you're a witness to something important," not "you saw something interesting"
> > - **Buy flowers** (1 gold) — she softens immediately; the transaction makes the conversation legitimate
> > - **Frame it as serving justice** — tell her the boy is in danger and you're trying to help. She responds to purpose.
> >
> > Once she opens up, she gives everything freely. No further checks needed.
> 
### The Newspaper Seller

> [!example] **The Newspaper Seller *(Nico Carbone)*
> ![[The newpaper seller.png]]
> - **Nico Carbone, the newspaper seller** *(NEE-koh kar-BOH-neh)* — Sharp-faced, northwest corner of the square. He heard the Fox say Pinocchio's name before Pinocchio said it himself — which he found odd at the time. He recognises a mark being worked. He watched long enough to see them go into the Inn together. He won't talk freely without a small transaction first.
>
> > [!tip] GM Note — Nico's Social Layer
> > Nico is transactional, not hostile. He gives information to people who give something first — that's how his corner of the world works.
> > - **Buy a newspaper** (1 gold). The moment money changes hands, he relaxes and answers everything directly and without embellishment.
> > - No Persuasion check needed after the purchase. He is not withholding information maliciously — he simply operates on small exchanges. Once paid, he is precise and reliable.
### The Postwoman

> [!example] **The Postwoman** *(Delia Conti)*
> ![[The Postwoman.png]]
> - **Delia Conti, the postwoman, mid-round** *(DEL-yah KON-tee)* — Moving through the square when it happened; she didn't stop. But she remembers the Fox clearly: *"Silver-topped cane, well-dressed, feigning a limp. Couldn't tell you what happened to the limp when they reached the corner."* Made a delivery to the cobbler's five minutes later and mentioned it in passing.
>
> > [!tip] GM Note — Delia's Social Layer
> > Delia needs no persuasion — she's helpful by default. The constraint is time: she has a route to finish and won't linger. Keep her answers brief and purposeful.
> >
> > Her most useful function is as a **pointer**. After sharing her own observation, she proactively suggests: *"If you want more, talk to the cobbler across the square — he's had dealings with those two before. The flower seller near the south gate saw the whole thing. And Nico on the north corner — he heard something I didn't."* She connects the party to witnesses they may not have found yet.

### The Police Officer

> [!example] **The Police Officer** *(Leopoldo)*
> ![[The Police Officer.png]]
> **The Police Officer (Leopoldo):** Can be encountered here or anywhere in town. He is not a villain, but he is distinctly unpleasant: he thinks little boys are troublemakers not worth worrying about, he is clearly more interested in the Fox and Cat as a career case (a bigger collar, better for his record), and he is visibly irritated by the party acting as amateur investigators stepping on a real investigation. Geppetto filed a formal report two days ago. Leopoldo logged it. He has conducted no investigation, made no enquiries, and followed up with no one — he considers Pinocchio's disappearance the expected consequence of a naive boy being taken in by obvious swindlers, and not worth the town guard's time. He has heard about the ruckus at the Inn and considers it the boys' fault for going in there in the first place. He answers direct questions, but every answer comes wrapped in faint contempt. **DC 13 Insight** suggests he knows more about the Fox and Cat's movements than he's letting on, and has not acted on it. He has also received reports of missing children from two nearby villages in the past month. He logged them. He did nothing. He considers it normal — children run away; it is not a matter for the town guard.

## The Savings Bank

> [!info] **The Savings Bank** *(west side of the square)*
> *A narrow two-storey building with a brass plate and a door that sticks. Inside, two clerks work behind a long wooden counter in an atmosphere of enforced calm. The ledgers are perfect. Nothing that happens here is surprising, which is exactly why they remember what didn't.*
>
> ![[The Banker.png]]
> **Key NPC: Isidoro Palmieri, the senior clerk** — Thin, precise, faintly aggrieved, and waiting for someone to ask the right question. Genuinely useful if treated with patience; less so if you rush him.
>
> **What players can learn here:**
> - The puppet-boy never arrived. The deposit was expected — Geppetto is a regular, reliable, same time most weeks. The absence has been noted in the log.
> - The clerk was at his window at approximately eight o'clock (he is always there at seven-fifty) and saw the boy. *Not coming toward the bank — on the east side of the square.* The wrong side entirely for someone heading to the bank. The boy was already heading away when the clerk spotted him.
> - **DC 11 Investigation:** A second clerk, separately consulted, adds: two strangers were walking alongside the boy — a fox with a cane and a cat who kept bumping into things. She watched them cross the square together heading east.

## The Cobblers

> [!info] **The Cobbler's Shop** *(north side of the square)*
> *A ground-floor shopfront with a worn step and a window full of lasts and leather. The cobbler is a broad, deliberate man who has worked this corner for twenty years and knows every face in the square. He is currently sitting behind his counter with a cup of tea going cold, waiting for the police to take a statement.*
>
> ![[The Cobbler.png]]
> **Key NPC: Rodolfo Amati, the cobbler** *(roh-DOL-foh ah-MAH-tee)* — Not given to drama, but quietly furious. He has dealt with the Fox and Cat before. He knows exactly who they are. He will point the party straight to the Inn — but he won't easily volunteer that he himself was swindled. That part is embarrassing.
>
> **What players can learn here:**
> - He did **not** see Pinocchio — his view of the square is partially blocked by the awning post to his left, and he was working through the relevant window of time.
> - The Fox and Cat he knows by sight. They have hit his shop before — a pair of shoes walked out the door while he was in the back. He has filed a complaint. The police know. Nothing has been done.
> - He heard about a ruckus at the Red Crawfish Inn this morning — a puppet-boy, a disputed bill, the boy running. He says it plainly: *"If you're looking for the boy, you want the Inn. East side of the square. The crawfish sign above the door."* This is the clearest and most direct pointer to the Inn in the entire investigation.
> - The Fox and Cat are **not** opportunists. They are known criminals with a pattern and a file in this town. The cobbler says this as established fact, without drama.
>
> > [!tip] GM Note — Rodolfo's Social Layer
> > Rodolfo will freely point the party to the Inn — that costs him nothing. But getting his *full* account (that the Fox and Cat robbed him personally, that the police did nothing, the depth of his ongoing fury) requires a bit more. He doesn't want to look like a victim in front of strangers.
> >
> > To unlock his complete story, the party needs one of the following:
> > - **DC 12 Persuasion** — standard approach; ask respectfully and he'll talk
> > - **Show they're also investigating the Fox and Cat** — if the players make clear they're pursuing the same criminals, his solidarity opens him up without a check needed
> > - **Acknowledge the injustice** — saying something like "the police did nothing?" lands. He relaxes visibly. Then he tells everything.
> >
> > His full account adds useful detail: the pattern, the file, the institutional failure. This makes the Fox and Cat feel like a real systemic problem rather than just a one-off swindle.

## The School

> [!info] **The School** *(north street, one block from the square)*
> *A tall building with high windows, presided over by a woman who has known every child in this neighbourhood for thirty years. The desks inside are in perfect rows. A spelling-chart on the wall. The smell of chalk and a particular quality of attentive quiet.*
>
> ![[The Schoolmistress.png]]
> **Key NPC: Serafina Conti, the schoolmistress** *(seh-rah-FEE-nah KON-tee)* — Warm and watchful, with a register that is never wrong. She sent word to Geppetto the moment she was certain. She knows her students well enough to know when something is wrong.
>
> **What players can learn here:**
> - Pinocchio was not at school. Noted in the register: *ABSENT — no message sent.* An unexplained absence is a serious matter.
> - **Lampwick is also absent.** And this worries Serafina differently — specifically. Lampwick has been distracted lately. *Obsessed* with the idea of running away to become a puppeteer. The Mangiafuoco posters have lit something in him; he's been talking about nothing else for weeks. His absences lately have felt less like truancy and more like preparation. Serafina does not think Lampwick was taken. She thinks Lampwick left of his own accord — she just doesn't know where, or whether Pinocchio went with him.
>
> > [!tip] GM Note — The Lampwick Thread
> > Lampwick ran away voluntarily to join Mangiafuoco's Puppet Theatre — that's where the trail leads, and the posters make the connection obvious in retrospect. The players can't and shouldn't resolve this in Chapter 1; it pays off in a later chapter when the party reaches the theatre and finds Lampwick inside it, willing but no longer entirely free. Don't let anyone pull this thread now. Serafina Conti's specific worry — "obsessed," the posters, the sense that his absences had started to feel like preparation — should simply sit in the room and be remembered later.

# The Inn

> [!warning] Scene Setup — The Bill
>
> The party pushes open the door of the Red Crawfish Inn. The room is low and amber-lit, smelling of old wine and fried oil. A carved wooden crawfish hangs above the door. There is a fire that smokes badly. Near the back, a corner table shows the remains of a large meal — three settings, two scraped clean, one barely touched. A wine glass has been knocked on its side and not righted. A door to the kitchen swings on a squeaking hinge.
>
> **Mastro Lucone, the innkeeper**, sees the party come in. He is a broad man in a stained apron, and he has been waiting. He does not wait for them to speak.
>
> *"You're working for the old woodcarver. Word gets around."*
>
> A pause while he takes them in with the expression of a man running a careful inventory.
>
> *"Twelve gold pieces. Wine, bread, a roast, and a dessert — for three. Seems to me that whoever's finding the puppet answers for what the puppet owes."*
>
> He moves to block the exit. He is not asking.

> [!example] The Innkeeper — Mastro Lucone *(📖 Book)*
>
> **Personality:** Not a villain — a working man who has been cheated once too often and has decided the only way to stop being cheated is to stop letting people leave. His anger is genuine; his logic (*you work for the father, you settle the son's tab*) is aggressive but consistent. He is not extracting blood money; he wants to be made whole. He will not be moved by argument alone, and he will not let anyone leave on a promise.
>
> ![[The Innkeeper.png]]
> **His account:** Once the situation has resolved, he gives it freely. The Fox and Cat arrived mid-morning with the boy. They ordered lavishly. The Fox talked through the whole meal, low and close to the boy's ear. Just before midday, the Fox rose and announced — publicly, in a voice that carried — that everything was *"taken care of."* Then he and the Cat were gone through the front door. Twenty minutes later, Lucone came to collect. *"The boy looked confused, not guilty. He kept saying the Fox had settled it — said it carefully, like saying it more clearly would make it true. He wasn't lying. I've seen liars. He believed it. Then I raised my voice, and people looked, and he ran. East."*
>
> **Mechanical stat block:** Use the **Mastro Lucone** stat block (see [[The Innkeeper]]). He fights to grapple, block exits, and create noise. He has the weight of a man who has spent thirty years hauling barrels and the *Righteous Fury* trait while he feels unacknowledged.

> [!example] The Innkeeper's Wife — Vittoria Lucone *(vit-TOR-yah loo-KOH-neh)*
>
> **Personality:** More reasonable than her husband, and she knows it. She is in the kitchen — through that squeaky door — and she will not let this go too far. She does not want blood in the taproom. She does not want the law at her own door. And she does not want her husband to embarrass himself in front of strangers. She is the safety valve.
>
> **The safety valve mechanic:** If the party is clearly losing — two or more characters down, or things turning ugly — Vittoria comes through the kitchen door, wipes her hands on her apron, and puts herself between her husband and the party.
>
> *"That is enough. Sit down, Lucio. These people didn't cheat you."*
>
> She does not end the negotiation. She ends the violence. Once she intervenes, the room settles — and the question of the bill remains, but the context has shifted. She saw everything that happened this morning and will say so, clearly, once the room is calm. She can also call off Taddeo; he listens to her in a way he does not listen to her husband.

> [!warning] Tutorial Combat — The Fight at the Red Crawfish
>
> **This is the chapter's tutorial fight.** It should happen. The escalation is forced and clear — Lucone blocks the exit before asking nicely, and the situation narrows. This is not optional combat. It is a fight that arrives without the party having to choose it.
>
> **The space — make it kinetic:**
> - Tables can be flipped for half cover (costs an Action)
> - Wine barrels near the bar are difficult terrain; a barrel can be knocked rolling (DC 12 Athletics, forces a Dexterity saving throw from anyone in its path)
> - The kitchen door swings open on its squeaky hinge at inopportune moments — a good moment to introduce the cook before he becomes a combatant
> - A low chandelier is just high enough to swing from (DC 13 Athletics; on success, the next attack has advantage)
> - **The kitchen doorway** is a tactical choke point once Taddeo enters the fight — his *Kitchen Fury* reaction means anyone stepping through it takes a free hit
>
> **Lucone's backup — the kitchen staff:**
>
> If things are going Lucone's way, he can call for backup. Two staff emerge:
>
> - **Taddeo Moretti, the chef** *(tah-DAY-oh moh-RET-tee)* (see [[The Chef]]) — Enormous, furious, and completely non-tactical. He comes out of the kitchen angry and stays angry. His CR is 1/2 and his STR is 17 — he hits hard. He has no loyalty to Lucone beyond the immediate situation. His *Kitchen Fury* reaction means the kitchen doorway becomes dangerous the moment he's on the field. He cannot be charmed or persuaded to stop while he's defending his kitchen.
>
> - **Rosa Ferrini, the waitress** *(ROH-zah feh-REE-nee)* (see [[The Waitress]]) — She fights because she has to, not because she wants to. CR 1/8, slightly above average DEX. Her attacks are halfhearted — a tray raised more as a shield than a weapon. A **DC 10 Persuasion** check mid-combat (used as an action) turns her from opponent to bystander. If any ally goes down, her *Reluctant Fighter* trait lets her Disengage for free. She is the encounter's soft exit and, if won over, a minor source of information about the morning.
>
> **The wife's intervention:** If the party is losing badly (two or more unconscious, or clearly overwhelmed), Vittoria comes through the door and stops it. This should feel like rescue, not failure.
>
> **Lucone's tell:** A **DC 14 Insight** check reveals he is both genuinely wronged *and* not above leveraging the situation. He could have stopped the boy at the door. He didn't — a party of armed strangers is easier to collect from than a puppet.

> [!tip] Post-Fight Resolution
>
> **The fight ends — however it ends. The bill remains.**
>
> The outcome of the fight affects *how* the party negotiates, not *whether* they have to. Lucone has calmed down. His wife is present. He still wants his twelve gold pieces.
>
> - **If the party won clearly:** Lucone is irritable but chastened. Negotiation DCs drop by 2. He is grudgingly respectful of people who hit back.
> - **If Vittoria intervened:** She is managing the conversation now. DC 14 Persuasion to reduce the bill to a partial payment plus a promise; **DC 11** if the party acknowledges the genuine wrong done to Lucone — the Fox and Cat cheated him, and that part is true.
> - **If the party lost:** Lucone is calmer now that he's made his point. Harder negotiation (DC 16), but possible. He may take collateral — a weapon, a pack — against the promise of full payment later.
>
> **Resolution options:**
> - **Pay in full (12gp):** Lucone accepts, mollified. He tells them everything. The party starts Chapter 2 lighter.
> - **Pay partially + Persuade:** Lucone grumbles but accepts if treated with respect.
> - **Work it off:** Three hours. Washing up, hauling firewood, scrubbing the floor. He watches them. During the work, a **DC 12 Perception** check notices a figure in a brown coat in the corner — very still, piece of paper in front of him, very small and very neat handwriting. That is Rabbit.
> - **Collateral:** Lucone takes something as a hold, creating a hook to return.
>
> **Regardless of outcome:** Once resolved, both Lucone and his wife are willing witnesses. The boy ran east. The Fox and Cat went the same way. The road leads out past the east gate toward the hills — and the Field of Miracles.

# Rabbit's Introduction
![[Rabbit.png]]
> [!example] Character: Rabbit *(Milne original / Disney)*
>
> **Personality:** Efficient, opinionated, perpetually exasperated. Expresses care through irritation. At his best in a crisis, because crisis rewards the skills he actually has. Currently making a list. The list is extremely accurate.
>
> **Why here:** Eeyore has been missing for two weeks. Rabbit has spent those two weeks travelling from town to town in the surrounding region — asking questions, following rumours, getting nowhere. They happened to be in Krat on the morning Pinocchio went missing. Pure coincidence. By the time the players encounter him, Rabbit has just returned from another fruitless circuit of the area and is preparing to head home. He is tired, worried, and quietly frightened in a way he will not name.
>
> He stopped at the Red Crawfish for a meal before his departure and arrived at exactly the wrong time. He witnessed the Fox and Cat work their scheme from a nearby table. He was not deceived. He simply sat down for lunch and watched something happen in front of him that he could not, in good conscience, ignore.
> When he heard you were looking for a puppet boy he had to help in a way he was hoping others would help him find Eeyore.

> [!tip] GM Note — Rabbit and Eeyore
> The Eeyore backstory — what happened, why Rabbit has been searching, what the past two weeks have been like — is best delivered on the road in Chapter 2, when the party is travelling together and there is time for it to breathe. In Chapter 1, Rabbit can mention he has been looking for a lost friend without going into detail. He will not volunteer the full story in a crowded inn. He is private, and it is painful, and there are more pressing things at hand.
>
> **What he knows:** Rabbit heard the Fox describe the Field of Miracles — the location, the method, the directions — and wrote it all down. He watched Pinocchio sit between the Fox and Cat, looking attentive and not suspicious enough. He watched the Fox and Cat slip out through the *side door* — not the front — without a coin changing hands. He watched Lucone come to the table. He watched the boy insist, carefully and sincerely, that the Fox had paid. He watched the boy run.
>
> *He noted which direction.*

> [!info] The Introduction Scene
>
> *The fight has resolved — however it resolved. There is a blue glow at the Inn door that fades as the door opens.*
>
> *"You're looking for the puppet-boy."*
>
> *A rabbit — brown coat, slightly rumpled, a piece of paper covered in very small neat writing in their hand — regards them with the expression of someone reserving judgement on whether competent people have arrived.*
>
> *"I was here that morning. I heard everything."*
>
> *He reads from his note.*
> 
> *"The Fox told the boy about a field — past the east gate, half a mile down the old road toward the hills — where if you bury coins in the earth and say the right words, they multiply overnight. He called it the Field of Miracles." A pause. "It isn't. The boy didn't know that."*
>
> *"The Fox and Cat left through the side door. No payment made— I watched. The innkeeper is not wrong about that. The boy clearly believed the Fox had settled it. He looked confused, and then frightened, and then he ran. He was still holding the purse when he went through the door. He went east."*
>
> *He tucks the paper into his coat with the decisive air of someone closing a meeting.*
>
> *"This Field of Miracles is located in the woods where I live, which is where the boy went. I can show you the way, maybe then my journey won't be wasted."*
>
> A beat.
>
> *"I assume you have horses. No? Hmm."*
>
> *He straightens his coat.*
>
> *"Then we walk."*

> [!tip] Rabbit's Role in Chapter 2
>
> Rabbit joins the party for the journey to and investigation of the Field of Miracles. He is practically useful — organised, observant, good at spotting the Fox and Cat's operational patterns, constitutionally incapable of letting an inconsistency pass unremarked. He is a helpful stranger with information and a practical reason to travel the same road. He is not a victim of this chapter's scheme and should not be played as one.
>
> **Key beats:**
> - He will not admit that any of this is emotionally affecting him. Sympathy for Pinocchio is expressed sideways — via information, practicality, a reluctance to let something unjust stand. If pressed, he might say: *"The boy was naive, not foolish. There's a difference."*
> - If the party handles something well — catches a detail he missed, defuses a situation gracefully — he will say *"that was competently done,"* in a tone that is functionally neutral and is the closest he gets to warmth.
> - A **DC 13 Insight** check suggests he has encountered the Fox and Cat's methods before, somewhere else, and that it did not end well. He does not volunteer this.
> - If anyone is genuinely kind to him — without making a fuss about it — he is briefly, unmistakably touched, and immediately changes the subject.
>
> **His notes:** The paper he was working on contains the Fox's directions to the Field of Miracles, descriptions of both criminals, an accurate timeline of this morning's events, and a note reading *"the boy is innocent — confirm on arrival."* It is more useful than anything else the party gathered this chapter.
>
> **His departure:** When the trail leads beyond the Field of Miracles — deeper into Pinocchio's world, further than Rabbit said he'd go — he stops. *"This is further than I said I'd go,"* he says, which is not quite the same as saying he won't. He makes sure the party has his notes before he leaves. He returns to the Hundred Acre Wood — though he will not say exactly how.
