# The World

The Lands of Albion is a place of fables and half-remembered stories, where magic is real and the line between fantastical and factual is often very thin. You will not find the creatures of more familiar tales here — no orcs, no goblins, no elves, no dwarves. In their place are two peoples: Man, and the Animals.

Man is the common folk of Albion — farmers, merchants, soldiers, scholars. Most people live in small towns and villages scattered across a land of forests, river valleys, and old roads worn down by centuries of cart wheels. Life is modest for most; there are no great empires here, only a patchwork of settlements, each governed by whoever is clever or wealthy or respected enough to take the role. Travel between towns takes time and is not always safe. A day’s ride might separate one village from the next; a week’s walk might separate it from anything you might call a city.

The major cities are another world entirely. They have universities, academies, and schools for those with magical gifts — provided those gifts are accompanied by enough gold to pay the tuition. Magic is not rare in Albion, but *trained* magic is expensive. A wizard who studied at one of the great academies commands far more respect, and far higher fees, than a village hedge-mage who learned from a grandmother’s handwritten notes. The gap between those with access to formal magical education and those without is one of the quiet injustices of Albion: not often discussed, but felt clearly by anyone on the wrong side of it.

Science is rarer still, and treated with something between indifference and mild suspicion. Word reaches the villages occasionally — usually through a traveller with a habit of embellishment — of buildings in distant places lit by neither candle nor spell, running on some mechanism no one can quite explain. You have never seen such a thing yourself. Few have. In a world where magic already fills the role that machinery might otherwise claim, there has been little pressure to develop the alternative. The academies train wizards, not engineers; the universities debate metaphysics, not mechanics. Science is something that happens elsewhere, if it happens at all.

The Animal folk are another matter. They walk upright, speak common, and live their lives much as Man does — trading, farming, keeping homes, raising families. But they are few. Most people have met no more than a handful in their lifetimes, and in smaller towns, one passing through is cause for stares and whispered comment. Animals tend to keep to themselves: a cottage on the edge of the wood, a farm a few miles from the nearest village, a trading post at a remote crossroads. They come and go on their own business and prefer not to linger where they are not welcome. In the cities, some have built reputations that earn them something closer to respect. In the countryside, suspicion is more common than courtesy.

There is no single reason for this distrust — it is old and diffuse, passed down in the way most unreasonable things are passed down: through habit, through folk stories, through the simple human tendency to be wary of whatever is not ordinary. The Animals have long since stopped expecting better, and mostly learned to work around it.

There is a third kind, if the old stories are to be believed — though most sensible people would tell you they are not. Fairy folk. Not seen in living memory, nor in any memory that can be traced with confidence. The legends say they once moved freely through the world, luminous and apt to meddle in mortal affairs — granting wishes to the deserving, or occasionally the undeserving, according to their own inscrutable judgment. The stories say they left. Fled, some versions have it, driven off by the slow spread of man into the wild places they favoured; that they crossed into other lands and sealed the way behind them. Wherever they went, they did not announce their departure, and they have not returned. Every generation or so, someone claims a sighting — a shimmer at the edge of the forest, a voice with no body, a wish that somehow, against all likelihood, came true. The claims are never verifiable. The proof never materialises. Most people treat fairies the way they treat old family curses or lucky charms: something to mention in passing, nothing to actually plan around.

## Something Has Changed

In the last year or two, there have been more missing persons reports than usual — more than the normal churn of runaways, debtors gone to ground, and travellers who misjudged the road after dark. Children, specifically. A boy from one village. A girl from another. The reports are spread enough across the land that no single town feels the pattern; each only feels its own grief. The rumours travel along trade routes, passed between merchants and carters and innkeepers in the same breath as the weather and the price of grain. A tension is building in the villages not felt in the big cities.

# Story Setup

Your party works out of a small bounty hunter agency — the kind of place that deals in the unglamorous end of law and order. Most of your work involves tracking down debtors who have missed their repayment dates,  and serving summons to people who have gone to some effort to avoid being found. The work is steady, if not exciting, and the agency takes their cut.

Lately it has been slow. Slow enough that the agency has been taking jobs that stretch the definition of “bounty hunting” — a stolen cart horse or goods, a merchant who needed someone with a serious expression to stand behind him during a difficult conversation, a run away bride. Any legitimate job, in other words.

The latest job is a request from the village of Krat, two days south. A shopkeeper there is looking for his missing boy. 

The man is offering everything he has.

That is the detail that stays with you. Not the amount — the amount is modest — but the way it is written: plainly, without flourish, in the handwriting of someone who does not write often. *This is my life savings. Please find my boy.* No negotiation. No conditions. Just a father who has run out of other options and is hoping someone will take the job seriously.

You leave in the morning.

# D&D... & You

> [!info] Terminology
> - **Party**: A team of adventurers
> - **PC**: Player Characters, these are the character you control (one each)
> - **NPC**: Non-player characters, these are the characters you meet and interact with and are controlled by the the DM
> - **DM**: Dungeon Master, this it the person who runs the sessions. They create and guide the story, control the NPCs, and adjudicate the rules
> - **Adventures**: These are the stories you play, they can be written by the DM or prewritten stories the DM guides you through. These could be exploring a castle or battling your way through a dungeon.
> - **Campaigns**: These are a collection of adventures that together tell a greater narrative arc.

> [!example] Rhythm of Play
> 1. The DM describes a scene
> 2. The players describe what their characters do
> 3. The DM decides how to resolve the characters actions and describes the result
>
> **Resolving Actions**
> If the players want to perform an action that would require skill or luck then they DM will decide on a **Check** for the player(s) to perform. The DM would pick an appropriate skill and the player would roll a 20 sided dice (D20) adding their skill modifier to the result. The DM compares the final result against a threshold based on the difficulty of the task and describes the outcome.
>
> **Difficulty**
> A typical difficulty scale would look like this:
> 
> | Task Difficulty   | Threshold or DC (difficulty class) |
> | ----------------- | --------- |
> | Very easy         | 5         |
> | Easy              | 10        |
> | Medium            | 15        |
> | Hard              | 20        |
> | Very Hard         | 25        |
> | Nearly Impossible | 30        |
> 
> ***Example***
> *A player is running away from a monster and runs down a steep set of stairs, they role a athletics check to see if they trip and fall, this would be classed as easy with a target of 10.*
> 
> *A player is running away from a monster and runs down a steep set of stairs covered in ice, they role an athletics check to see if they fall, this would be classed as hard or very hard with a target of 22.*

> [!abstract] Your Character
> 1. **Pick your character class:** Do you want to be a fighter, or a magic user, or a healer?
> 2. **Create their personal details:** What’s their name, what do they look like, why are they working as a bounty hunter? To fit in with the story/world all the players are human.

> [!failure] Things to ignore
> Dungeon & Dragons has a lot of rules, in the interest of time, ease, and most importantly fun there are many rules we are going to ignore. In certain scenarios or of a particular chapter we may use some for a short time but we will go over them if it comes up.
> - Food, water, materials, ammo, crafting - we aren’t going to track these, the archer spending 20 mins to by arrows in every village or not be able to do anything because they don’t have any arrow left is just boring. We don’t need the magic user to describe making potions they need for a spell they may or may not use the next day.
> - Travel speed - As the DM I will just tell you “you’ve been travelling for 30 mins when…” or “the next town is about an hours journey away”
> - Battle maps and miniatures - Most of the combat will be within range of the enemy, we can use common sense for positioning e.g. the fighter runs up and punches a skeleton, then the magic user casts a spell that causes a 10ft explosion we all know the fighter would get hit
> - Money - If we use money in that adventure we will only use gold (gp), we don’t need to deal with how many of each coin you have or different currencies.
> - Weight/carry limits - whilst I won’t let you pickup something that weighs as much as a car working out how much your weapons and armour weigh is not going to serve the story.
> - Experience points - to simplify things between adventures we will have short interludes where you will level by set amounts, e.g. after the first adventure you may go from level 1 to level 3, get rewards and have a chance to buy things for the next adventure.
