# Campaign Overview: The Wooden Boy

A D&D campaign rooted in the world of Pinocchio, interwoven with the characters of the Hundred Acre Wood. The campaign follows a party of investigators through a world where children have been disappearing — taken by a network of intermediaries, all connected by a symbol nobody has yet named.

---

## Prologue: Setup

**Location:** Geppetto's workshop, Krat.

Geppetto is introduced: a woodcarver, elderly, grieving. His workshop is full of puppets. Photographs of a dead son — Carlo — are on the mantel. His wife died of the same illness years before. He does not speak of either easily.

Pinocchio came to life one night. Not through science, not through craft — through something Geppetto does not fully understand and does not question. A wooden boy who breathes, who feels, who lies badly and knows it. Geppetto loves him completely.

Then Pinocchio disappeared.

The players are hired to find him.

---

## Chapter 1: Around Town (Krat)

An investigation chapter. Pinocchio disappeared two days ago on a simple errand — bank, then school — and never came home. Geppetto has reported it to the town watch; no investigation has followed. The players piece together what happened through witnesses, shopkeepers, and small details scattered across the town.

The trail leads to the Red Crawfish Inn, where the Fox and Cat swindled Pinocchio out of Geppetto's weekly earnings and sent him east toward the Field of Miracles. A second thread runs underneath: Lampwick, the baker's boy next door, is also missing. He wasn't taken — he went willingly, drawn by the Mangiafuoco posters that covered every post in town.

The chapter closes when the party meets Rabbit, who witnessed the whole scheme from a corner table and wrote it down. He knows where Pinocchio went. Rabbit is in Krat because Eeyore went missing two weeks ago — he and what friends from the Hundred Acre Wood he could gather have been travelling the nearby towns asking questions and finding nothing. He was in Krat the morning Pinocchio disappeared by coincidence, and is now preparing to head home empty-handed. Most of the Eeyore and Rabbit backstory is best delivered on the road in Chapter 2.

**Milestone:** None — the party begins here.

---

## Chapter 2: The Field of Miracles

The Fox and Cat are running a crop scheme in a clearing deep within the Hundred Acre Wood — convincing desperate people to bury their coins in the earth in exchange for promises of overnight multiplication. The Wood, normally alive with enchanted animals, is unnaturally quiet. The animals who live there are gone.

**Two separate operations, same location:** The Fox and Cat chose the clearing because the Wood is remote and unpatrolled. They did not know — or did not care — that a separate crew of weasel enforcers had come through the Wood to capture the Pooh characters. The weasel operation was organised, purposeful, and nothing to do with the Fox and Cat's con. The fact that both operations happened in the same place at roughly the same time suggests someone else is paying close attention to the Wood — and that what lives there is considered valuable.

**Roo:** He survived by hiding. Small enough to stay in the undergrowth and still while the weasels swept through, he was not found. He watched everything happen and does not fully understand what he saw. He is at the edge of the clearing when the players arrive, frightened and alone.

The Fox and Cat escape before the players can stop them. On the body of a dead weasel enforcer, the players find a docket with a symbol pressed into the wax seal: something circular, like a wheel, like a spoke pattern. Nobody recognises it yet.

**Milestone:** Roo returns to the Wood (or travels with the party, depending on the players).

---

## Chapter 3: The Puppet Theatre of Mangiafuoco

**Tone:** Gothic, sad, obsession. The theatre was once beautiful.

When the players arrive, the Puppet Theatre of Mangiafuoco is ruined — collapsed sections, scorched wood, broken machinery. It has not been a theatre for some time.

**What actually happened:** Mangiafuoco was not working with Him. He was stealing children and keeping them — to make his puppets. He wanted living material, living inspiration, living company for his creations. He came and destroyed the theatre as punishment for this betrayal, took everyone: the children, Lampwick, and Pinocchio, who had arrived looking for a way home. Mangiafuoco survived. He has not recovered.

**Additional DM context:** Eeyore was taken before this chapter — before the campaign begins — delivered directly to Pleasure Island as the Coachman's initial research subject into natural magical transformation. He was never brought to the theatre. When the Coachman sent his weasels to the Puppet Theatre, he had also instructed Mangiafuoco that he wanted any animals — useful to his research or as gifts for the heads of his operations such as the Ringmaster and the Piper. This is why Kanga was targeted alongside the children.

**Structure — Resident Evil mansion:** The players enter through the main hall. Most internal doors are locked. Keys are hidden in accessible rooms and must be found before deeper areas of the theatre can be reached. The layout rewards exploration and punishes rushing. Each unlocked area adds detail and dread: a costume room where children's clothes have been preserved and labelled, a workshop full of half-finished puppet bodies, a gallery of portraits of children who were "inspirations." The final confrontation is on the stage itself — Mangiafuoco as the star of his own tragedy, with the full set behind him and puppet automatons as his cast.

**The automatons:** Mangiafuoco's failed creations still walk the ruins, operating on old instructions, performing fragments of shows that no longer exist. They are not malicious. They are broken things doing what they were made to do. Fighting them is sad if the players are paying attention.

**Mangiafuoco:** Found on the stage, obsessing over plans and half-finished frames. He is dangerous but pitiable — a craftsman who built something alive once and cannot stop trying to repeat it. He believes Pinocchio is his masterwork, stolen from him. He will fight to protect his remaining work.

He is completely unreachable on the question of what was done to him and the children. He refers to his destroyer only as "Him" or "He" — never by name, never with explanation, always with a strange fearful reverence that the players will notice. If pressed, he shuts down entirely on the subject. He cannot or will not say the name.

**Boss fight:** Mangiafuoco — mechanical traps, puppet soldiers, the full stage as his arena. He is not evil. He is broken.

**Kanga:** She has been imprisoned in the theatre to look after the children Mangiafuoco kidnapped — he needed someone to keep them fed and calm while he worked, and Kanga's nature made her the obvious choice. She is the emotional heart of the chapter: steady, frightened but holding it together, focused entirely on the welfare of the children in her care. When the fight ends, her first concern is the children, not herself. Once the chapter resolves, she takes on the role of base camp caretaker — managing the Hundred Acre Wood as a safe house, caring for rescued children as they are brought back through subsequent chapters.

**The office (reward for defeating Mangiafuoco):** Defeating him yields a key to his private office, a room at the back of the theatre that has not been opened since the destruction. It is a room of beautiful obsession turned ruin: notebooks everywhere, diagrams pinned over diagrams, the detritus of a man who spent years trying to understand something he couldn't name.

On the wall: a large board covered in red string. Mangiafuoco, in his lucid moments, had been piecing something together. The string connects:

- A **poster for THE WANDERING CIRCUS OF MARVELS** — colourful, professional, recently printed. The wagon wheel logo on the poster has been circled in red. It looks exactly like a circus tent from above.
- **Missing children notices from Molino Verde** — several of them, pinned in a row. Each one features a small engraved image of the village. In the background of every image, a water mill stands on the river. The water wheel has been circled on each notice.
- A **shipping company advertisement**, showing stacked crates decorated with rope designs. One of the rope patterns — an eight-spoke design used as a decorative flourish — looks exactly like a ship's wheel. It has been circled.
- At the centre of all the red string: a scrawled note in large, frantic handwriting — *"make HIM pay"* — and pinned at the absolute centre of the board: a plain business card. No text. No name. No address. Just a black wagon wheel symbol printed on white card.

The players now understand: all three threads — the Circus, Molino Verde, the harbour with the shipping operation — are connected to the same mark. And whoever holds that card is the thing Mangiafuoco will not name.

**This is the pivot point.** The three leads are visible. The players choose which to follow first.

**Milestone:** Kanga returns to the Wood.

---

## Non-Linear Investigation Arc (Chapters 4, 5, 6)

After Chapter 3, the players have three leads from the board in Mangiafuoco's office. They can pursue them in any order.

Each chapter:
- Has a distinct antagonist and minion type
- Saves some children, but reveals there are more elsewhere
- Plants a piece of the puzzle pointing toward the Coachman, the Grand Coach Company, and Pleasure Island
- Features the wagon wheel symbol in a context where it reads as something else entirely

---

## Chapter 4: The Wandering Circus of Marvels

**Tone:** Glittering surface, hollow underneath. The horror of performance without joy.

The Wandering Circus of Marvels has arrived near Krat for a season. It is impressive and loud and aimed at children.

**Antagonists: Fenwick and Celestine — The Ringmasters.** A male/female duo who have operated together for so long they have become a single entity in performance. They are best friends and partners — nothing more, but utterly devoted to each other, willing to do anything for the other without question or hesitation. Everything they do is theatrical and excessive: entrances are grand, exits are grander, even a simple conversation becomes a performance. Fenwick is the showman — top hat, sweeping gestures, a voice designed to fill the largest tent, incapable of saying in ten words what he can say in fifty. Celestine is the operator — costumed with more elegance than excess, a ledger always nearby, the one who actually decides what happens to people. They finish each other's sentences, not as a trick but as a genuine habit of years.

> *"We always say—"*
> *"—the audience doesn't need to understand the mechanism—"*
> *"—only the effect."*

They are utterly ruthless underneath. The cruelty is not personal — it is simply the practical side of running a business, and they have made peace with that so thoroughly they no longer notice it.

**Their single pressure point:** Any slight against their artistry or the quality of the show is the fastest way to violence. A comment that the performance was mediocre, that a trick was badly executed, that something could have been done better — this is the one thing that pierces their professional calm completely. Conversely, genuine admiration for the show is the surest way to their good side. Players who lead with flattery about the spectacle will find them warm and forthcoming. Players who question it will find the temperature drop very quickly.

**Minions:** Acrobat enforcers, trained creatures used as weapons, clowns whose fighting style is profoundly unsettling.

**The children here** are exhibited as acts. They haven't been transformed yet, but they're being broken in — fear and repetition and the withholding of food until a performance is good enough. They are not visibly suffering. That is the point.

**Tigger:** Being exhibited as a novelty act. Stripped of his bounce — not magically, but through confinement and routine and being told, repeatedly, that what he is naturally is inconvenient. Finding him and freeing him is the emotional core of the chapter.

**The connection:** Fenwick and Celestine are business partners of the Coachman's. Children who perform well enough go to Pleasure Island. Children who don't are "transferred." The players will hear this phrase but not understand it yet.

**The symbol:** The wagon wheel appears as the Circus logo — specifically designed to look like a tent from above. Players who notice it may simply assume it's a circus trademark.

**What the players learn:** The Circus is a sorting facility. Children with potential go to Pleasure Island. The ones who don't — Celestine doesn't say what happens to them. She doesn't need to. Fenwick changes the subject before the players can press it, which is its own answer.

**Milestone:** Tigger returns to the Wood.

## Chapter 5: The Pied Piper

**Tone:** Eerie, heist-like, morally complex. A wrong done in good faith is still a wrong.

The players arrive in Molino Verde, a town with a water mill on the river — the same village waterwheel that Mangiafuoco circled on his missing-children notices. Something is immediately off.

**The town under trance:** Most townspeople go about their daily routines apparently normally — friendly, functional, making conversation. But the moment anyone mentions the Piper, the Mayor, what happened to the children, or anything connected to any of these — they shift. Eyes go wide and flat. Affect becomes robotic. They speak in the same practised phrases:

> *"The Mayor is a great man. Everyone loves the Mayor."*
> *"He saved the children. They are somewhere safe now."*
> *"There are no missing children here. The Mayor would know."*

If the players push back or try to argue the point, the townspeople become suddenly aggressive — and call for the rats. The trance is a side effect of long exposure to the pipe's enchantment, not an intentional cruelty. Adults have been saturated by weeks of ambient music. Children, more susceptible, simply follow.

**The Piper — the Mayor:** He has taken up residence in the Mayor's Mansion at the centre of town, the building with the large water wheel on the river behind it. He is not hiding. He is not defensive. He is completely unaware the players pose any threat — he is a man who has finally received the adoration he always believed he deserved, and he is enjoying it. He holds periodic "town meetings" from the mansion's balcony, playing a brief passage of music to address the crowd. These function as trance refreshes. The players must plan around his schedule and avoid being present during a performance — anyone caught in range of the pipe's music during a balcony address must make a Wisdom saving throw or fall into the trance for the remainder of the scene.

He genuinely believes he is doing good. The Grand Coach Company told him he was leading children to a school, a retreat, somewhere they would be safe and happy. He was paid well. He did not ask enough questions. He is not a villain by nature. He is responsible anyway.

**The rats:** The Piper's enforcers are rats — standing roughly half the height of a person, wearing clothes, organised and intelligent in the way the weasel enforcers or the Pooh characters are. They are the Piper's eyes and ears across the entire town: in the walls, on the rooftops, running errands, monitoring the streets. They enforce quietly and efficiently. The players will become aware of them gradually — a face at a high window, scurrying sounds in the walls of wherever they sleep, a figure on a rooftop that shouldn't be there. Once the Piper is alerted to a threat, the rats respond as a coordinated unit.

**The secret NPC:** One person in town has been pretending to be under the trance since the beginning. They noticed something was wrong immediately and made the decision to fake compliance rather than resist openly. They have been watching and waiting — and they have already found one ally: Christopher Robin got to them early, recognised they weren't truly enchanted, and has been hiding in their home ever since. When the players arrive and start asking the wrong questions, this NPC identifies them as potential allies and approaches them somewhere private — an alley, a locked back room — speaking quickly and quietly.

Their home becomes the planning base for the rest of the chapter. Christopher Robin, who has had weeks to observe the Piper's routine and the mansion layout from inside the town, is the party's best source of intelligence. The NPC knows which rats patrol which routes and where specific items can be found. Neither can help directly without exposing themselves, but together they are the party's intelligence source throughout.

**The heist structure:** The pipe is the key to everything — without it, the trance lifts, the rats scatter, and the nightly collections stop. But the players cannot simply walk in and take it. They need to prepare. The secret NPC identifies three things the party must acquire before the break-in can work:

- **Rat uniforms** — stolen from the rats' laundry. Disguises that allow movement through town without triggering the rats' alert network.
- **The mansion blueprint** — in the town records office, which has its own rat guard. Getting inside and finding the right document without being noticed is its own small operation.
- **A distraction item** — something specific that will hold the Piper's attention during his evening performance, creating a window to move through the mansion undetected. The secret NPC knows what he's vain about; the item plays to that.

Once everything is assembled, the players execute the break-in.

**The moral complexity:** If the players make the Piper understand the truth — confront him with evidence of what actually happens to the children he delivers — he can be redeemed. He can also be broken by it. Both outcomes are real. The campaign does not require his death. A redeemed Piper can be a resource in later chapters.

**The symbol:** It appears on the deed the Piper was given when he accepted the contract — a company stamp, circular, like a wheel. It has been on his desk the whole time. He never looked at it closely. He looked at the money.

**Winnie the Pooh** is the only Hundred Acre Wood character held in the mansion. When Christopher Robin followed the music into town, Pooh followed Christopher Robin — as he does. The Piper, who fancies himself a patron of the arts, was delighted by the little bear: Pooh sings when asked and seems content enough, which the Piper takes as validation. He has no idea Pooh wants to go home. Pooh is largely unbothered by his situation and mainly wants to know if there is honey at the base camp.

**Christopher Robin** is not in the mansion. He recognised early that the music was wrong and made the choice to resist rather than follow. He found the one person in town who wasn't truly enchanted and has been in hiding ever since, observing and mapping. He has had weeks to study the Piper's routine, the mansion's layout, and the rats' patrol routes, and is the party's best source of intelligence in the chapter. He takes the news of what happened to the Wood hardest, not dramatically, but in the quiet way of someone who was supposed to be old enough to prevent things like this.

**Fox and Cat** are staying in the mansion as guests. They arrived early in the Piper's tenure, quickly assessed the situation, and have been living in considerable comfort ever since — good rooms, good food, the Piper's goodwill — in exchange for lavishing him with praise and telling him his performances are magnificent. They are freeloaders in the most elegant possible sense. They know exactly what the Piper is doing and don't care. They are not working for the Coachman here — they are simply working the situation. The players will encounter them here, which can play as a comic scene, a tense standoff, or a negotiation depending on how previous meetings have gone.

**What the players learn:** The Grand Coach Company has agents operating across the entire land, hiring intermediaries. This is not a local operation. It is not even a regional one.

**Milestone:** Pooh and Christopher Robin return to the Wood — Pooh from the mansion, Christopher Robin from the hidden shelter.

## Chapter 6: Fisherman's Village & Inside Monstro

**Tone:** Two distinct halves — a morally grey harbour town, then Robinson Crusoe survival inside something enormous.

**Part 1 — The Village:** The party follows the trail of missing children to the coast. A fishing village: weathered, busy, not entirely honest. The harbormaster is running a smuggling operation, using children as cheap dock labour and loading the ones who are "ready" onto boats that leave at night. The symbol is carved into the crates on the smuggling boats — at the dock, it looks exactly like a ship's wheel, or possibly a standard dock marking. It has been here for years. Nobody has questioned it.

The players need to identify the operation, find proof, and stop the boats before the next shipment goes out to sea.

**Monstro:** Something goes wrong. The whale appears. The party — along with a faction of weasel enforcers who were on the smuggling boats — both end up inside the whale.

**Part 2 — Inside Monstro:** The whale's belly is vast. Other swallowed people have been here long enough to build a makeshift settlement from salvaged timber and cargo. The interior is a survival environment: dim light, the smell of brine and digestion, the sound of the whale's breathing all around.

The players must manage survival challenges (food, shelter, orientation, the whale's movements) while also dealing with the weasel faction, who are trying to do the same thing and have no interest in cooperation. The weasels, stripped of their authority and their boats, are dangerous and increasingly desperate — but they also know more about the Grand Coach Company's operations than they've let on, and that information has a price.

To escape, the party must make the whale sneeze — fire, explosive cargo, a sufficiently coordinated effort. This requires gathering resources and possibly dealing with the weasel problem first.

**Named characters inside:**
- **Captain Ahab** — obsessed, not entirely sane, but genuinely useful. He has been inside long enough to map portions of the whale's interior. He considers this his greatest quarry and has made a kind of peace with that which is not healthy.
- **Ishmael** — practical, kind, the person actually keeping Ahab functional. He is relieved to see new arrivals who seem competent.
- **Owl** — from the Hundred Acre Wood. He was swallowed months ago and has been living comfortably, taking careful notes about the interior, completely unbothered. He considers this an educational experience and has filled several notebooks. He is the most knowledgeable person in the whale.

**What the players learn:** From cargo manifests on the swallowed boats, from interrogating the weasels, and from the crates themselves: the children are being taken to an island. The company that owns the crates is the Grand Coach Company. The wagon wheel symbol — which they have now seen on a docket, a circus logo, a Piper's contract, and carved into shipping crates — is the same mark every time. The picture is complete enough to act on.

**Milestone:** Owl returns to the Wood.

---

## Chapter 7: Pleasure Island

**Tone:** The horror of desire. A place that gives children exactly what they want until it doesn't.

The players now know where to go. Pleasure Island is real, accessible, and not hidden — it is a destination that children are brought to willingly, or nearly so.

**Day:** Theme park atmosphere. Seductive and fun. The horror is slow — children choose to stay, encouraged to indulge every appetite. Sweets, games, no rules, no adults telling them what to do. The machinery underneath is invisible if you don't look.

**The Donkey Curse:** One player gets marked early — Constitution saving throws at random moments, or they briefly transform: antlers, a tail, hooves. It is reversible. It is also a ticking clock.

**Night:** The processing areas beneath the island. Children who have been here long enough have started to change. Not completely — not yet — but the signs are there.

**Pinocchio:** The party finds him here. He is brave and impulsive and genuinely good, and he has been trying to resist the island's pull since he arrived. He has not fully succeeded, and he knows it. Meeting the party is the thing that gives him something to hold onto.

**The Coachman:** Cold, charming, utterly sure of himself. He does not think of what he does as cruelty. He thinks of it as correction. Children are unruly, unproductive, wasteful. He is making them useful. He presents this with perfect calm, as though it were obvious. He is the most frightening antagonist in the campaign because he is not wrong about the children being difficult — he is simply monstrous in his conclusion.

**The fight:** The Coachman is not a straightforward combat encounter. He has layers — staff, magical defences, the island's own machinery. The players will need to disrupt his operation, free Pinocchio, and get the remaining children out.

**The Donkey Curse begins to lift** when the Coachman's power over the island breaks.

**The revelation:** In the Coachman's records — his actual files, meticulously kept — the players find a contract. Geppetto's name. A debt, described in dry commercial language. An agreement to deliver Pinocchio. Dated years ago. Before Pinocchio existed.

Geppetto knew. He made the deal before Pinocchio was ever born.

**Eeyore:** He wandered onto the island at some point looking for his friends. He found the games uninteresting and the food too sweet and has been sitting in a corner watching the other children enjoy themselves, waiting for someone to notice him. He is the last Pooh character found. He is fine. He is always fine. He just needs a ride home.

**Milestone:** Eeyore returns to the Wood. The Wood is complete.

## Chapter 8: Return to Krat — The Workshop

**Tone:** Familiar locations made strange. Something is wrong with the shape of things.

The party returns to Krat with Pinocchio. Geppetto has already arrived — impossibly fast, given where they last had any trace of him. He is at the workshop. The door is unlocked. He seems glad to see them.

The players move through the Chapter 1 locations again: the same streets, the same NPCs, new information. Something has changed about Geppetto. People who knew him seem uncertain. Small things are off: the timeline of his arrival, the state of the workshop, the things he says about Carlo.

**The secret lab:** Beneath the workshop, accessed through a hidden floor, is Geppetto's real work. Not Ergo-powered. Not petrification. Just grief made mechanical over decades. Carlo died of consumption. So did his mother. Geppetto has spent his whole adult life trying to replicate, through craft and mechanism, what the Blue Fairy did through something else entirely — forcing life into dead matter. He was never supposed to succeed. Pinocchio proved it was possible. Pinocchio is, in Geppetto's mind, the key.

**The confrontation:** Geppetto does not try to run or fight immediately. He tries to reason with the players. He is not a monster. He is a broken man who loves his dead son more than the living one, and he has been living in that love so long he cannot see what it has cost. The roleplay here is heavy. He should be sympathetic. He should be wrong.

**Boss Fight 1:** Geppetto — mechanical arms built into the workshop, puppet-soldiers activated from below, desperation and grief making him dangerous. He fights to protect his work. He is not trying to win.

## Chapter 9: Carlo

**Tone:** Tragedy. Catharsis. Earned emotion.

The machine activates — wrong, partial, desperate. Geppetto triggered it during the fight, or just before. Carlo is resurrected.

He is confused. He is in pain. He has been brought back wrong — not evil, not monstrous, but incomplete. He remembers fragments: his father's hands, the smell of sawdust, something about the cold. He does not understand what he is or where he is.

**Stage 1:** He speaks. He asks for his father. He sees Geppetto on the ground — hurt, unconscious, restrained — and he attacks the players. He is not trying to harm them specifically. He is trying to get to his father.

**Stage 2:** The machine overloads. His form becomes unstable. He is only pain and instinct. The fight becomes survival, not combat — the players are not killing him, they are enduring him while they find another solution.

**Stage 3 — The Peaceful Ending (optional, earned):** If the players have gathered something across the campaign — a token of the Blue Fairy's presence, Pinocchio's freely given consent, something that required effort and attention and care — they can end it peacefully. Not happily. Peacefully. Carlo can be allowed to rest. The machine can be stopped cleanly. It requires all of those things and a choice.

**Resolution:** Pinocchio decides what happens. This is his story. He is the one who lost a brother he never knew. He is the one who was made to replace someone else. Whatever he chooses — if the players have given him the chance to choose — the campaign ends with him. The players witness it.

## Recurring Threads

### The Fox & The Cat

Independent contractors — not loyal to the Coachman, but often hired by him, and by the Circus, and briefly by the Piper's backers. They appear across multiple chapters because they are following the same trail of missing children the players are, from the other direction. Each time the party encounters them, they have levelled alongside the party to approximately the same CR.

They sometimes tip the players off when it benefits them — to weaken a rival, not out of conscience. The party should never fully trust them, but may find them occasionally useful.

- **Chapter 2:** Running the Field of Miracles scheme in the Wood clearing. Entirely separate from the weasel operation happening nearby — they were there first, and the weasels ignored them.
- **Chapter 3:** They originally sold Lampwick to Mangiafuoco (a side gig — Mangiafuoco paid well for willing subjects, and the Fox considered Lampwick enthusiastic enough to count as willing).
- **Chapter 4:** Working for the Circus.
- **Chapter 5:** Guests of the Piper — staying in the mansion in comfortable rooms in exchange for telling him he is a genius. Not working for anyone; working for themselves. They arrived early in the Piper's tenure, assessed the situation rapidly, and have been living extremely well ever since. The players will find them here. This can play as a comic encounter, a tense standoff, or a negotiation depending on how previous meetings have gone.
- **Chapter 6:** Working for the harbour smugglers.
- **Chapter 7:** Possibly captured on Pleasure Island themselves — or possibly working for the Coachman right until the end. The party may never get a clean answer.

---

### The Blue Fairy

She cannot act directly. Her wish was bound to Geppetto's terms — she granted what he asked without fully understanding his hidden intent. Now she is bound: she cannot unmake Pinocchio, and she cannot help him directly. What she can do is guide others.

She manifests as a blue butterfly made of light. She appears briefly at liminal moments — a door just opened, a path chosen correctly, a child just saved. She never saves the day. She never lingers. She dissolves quickly, as though staying longer would cost her something.

- **End of Chapter 1:** A blue butterfly drifts near the party at the edge of town, then dissolves into dust. Rabbit is standing there. He does not comment.
- **After each child rescue:** She may briefly appear, then vanish.
- **Chapter 7, after defeating the Coachman:** She appears clearly, stays long enough for Pinocchio to see her. He asks who she is. She says something simple. Then she's gone.

---

### Why Were the Pooh Characters Taken?

The Hundred Acre Wood is an enchanted grove — talking animals, naturally magical, their natures more fixed and vivid than ordinary creatures. The Coachman discovered that proximity to enchanted creatures accelerates the Pleasure Island transformation. They function as catalysts for the donkey curse — and are valuable circus specimens in their own right.

Eeyore was the first taken — before the campaign begins, before the weasels ever came to the Wood. The Coachman had identified him as a naturally magical creature and took him directly to Pleasure Island as an initial research subject, to study how transformation works in a being whose nature is already fixed. Eeyore's disappearance is what first alerted the Hundred Acre Wood that something was wrong. It is why Rabbit had already been searching the surrounding towns for two weeks by the time the campaign opens.

Roo survived because he was small enough to hide in the undergrowth and stay still while the weasels swept through. He was not found. This is also why he was still in the Wood clearing when the players arrived in Chapter 2 — frightened, alone, and not yet understanding what had happened.

Pooh was not part of the original operation at all. He was in the Wood when the weasels came, but had already wandered off following a smell — almost certainly honey — and was not present during the raid. He subsequently followed Christopher Robin toward the music of the Pied Piper, because Pooh follows his friends without asking why. The Piper kept him as a novelty — charming, content-seeming, apparently happy to sing on request. His absence from the Wood is a coincidence of character rather than a deliberate act of the Coachman's operation.

---

### Geppetto and the Coachman

Geppetto made a deal with the Coachman years before Pinocchio existed.

Carlo and his mother died of consumption. Geppetto, in the depth of his grief, learned that the Coachman had knowledge of living transformation — the process he uses on boys at Pleasure Island — and believed he could reverse-engineer it to restore Carlo. In exchange for access to that knowledge, Geppetto built automata and puppet-workers for the Coachman's operations. Skilled work. Valuable work.

The deal went bad. The Coachman's magic only works in one direction and cannot restore the dead. By the time Geppetto understood this, he was too deeply complicit to simply walk away.

When Pinocchio came to life through the Blue Fairy, the debt came due. The Coachman wants Pinocchio to perfect his transformation process — creating obedient, living puppets rather than unruly, unpredictable donkeys. Pinocchio, to the Coachman, is proof of concept.

Geppetto's lab is not powered by magic fuel. It is powered by grief, guilt, and mechanical obsession. He has been building toward something for thirty years and calling it love.

---

### The Symbol

The wagon wheel is the Coachman's mark. It appears everywhere his operations touch. But it is always presented in a context where it reads as something else entirely.

- **Chapter 1:** Small print on the Mangiafuoco posters. A shipping mark on the noticeboard notice. Nobody looks twice.
- **Chapter 2:** Pressed into the wax on a docket found on a dead weasel enforcer. Just a mark.
- **Chapter 3:** The pivot point. Mangiafuoco's office board connects three separate instances — a circus logo that looks like a tent from above, a water wheel in the background of missing-children notices, a decorative rope design on a shipping advertisement that looks like a ship's wheel. At the centre: a plain business card with only a black wagon wheel on it.
- **Chapter 4:** The Circus logo, which the players now recognise from the board.
- **Chapter 5:** The water mill behind the Mayor's Mansion — the same waterwheel circled on the missing-children notices in Mangiafuoco's office. Also on the Piper's contract: a company stamp he never examined closely.
- **Chapter 6:** Carved into crates on the smuggling boats. A ship's wheel? A dock marking? The players have now seen this one before too.
- **Chapter 7:** Everywhere, unmistakably. The Coachman's brand. Every previous sighting connects.

### Jiminy Cricket

In this campaign, Jiminy Cricket is a real cricket — Pinocchio's pet, kept in a small jar. Not magical. Not talking. Just a cricket.

He was dropped somewhere during Pinocchio's capture. The players may find an empty jar at the Field of Miracles, still with a few dried grass stalks inside. On Pleasure Island, when the party finds Pinocchio, he still has the cricket — kept safe through everything. The jar is slightly cracked. The joke is that the "conscience" is just a bug, and Pinocchio kept it alive anyway.

The Hundred Acre Wood serves as the party's home base between chapters. Each time a Pooh character is found and returned, the party levels up. The Wood is quiet and depleted when the campaign begins — more characters return as the campaign progresses.
