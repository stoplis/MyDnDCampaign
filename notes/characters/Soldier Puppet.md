# Soldier Puppet

The largest of Mangiafuoco's automatons — seven feet of painted wooden plate armour, designed for the back of the stage to suggest an army. One arm ends at the shoulder: nothing there, the socket raw unfinished timber. The paint on its chest is chipped and faded. It moves slowly, in the deliberate way of something heavy that has decided not to hurry, because it has calculated that it does not need to.

The Soldier Puppet is the most dangerous enemy in the theatre in terms of raw force. It hits hard. It is difficult to move or destabilise. It does not tire, does not panic, does not hesitate. Its weakness is its pace — it cannot close ground quickly — and in open ground, players who maintain range and keep moving have time to act. The theatre's corridors and cluttered rooms take that advantage away. The Soldier Puppet was built for a stage, and the stage's cramped architecture works in its favour.

## Tactics

The Soldier Puppet advances in a straight line toward the nearest visible target and makes a single Maul attack — devastating on a hit, and a prone result gives any nearby Chorus Puppets immediate advantage. Players attempting to knock it prone or push it back will find it ignores both with Immovable. Do not flank it with two players standing still: one strike is sufficient to end a standing player's turn. The correct approach is sustained ranged damage, or drawing it toward the burned floor section in the Workshop where the soft boards may slow it.

Two Soldier Puppets stand motionless at the far end of the Workshop, facing the charred wall. They do not turn until the players are halfway across the room.

> [!tip] GM Note — The Missing Arm
> Do not draw attention to the missing arm as a weakness. It is not one. The Soldier Puppet uses one arm and one arm is enough. The missing limb is there to be noticed and then prove meaningless — an object lesson in not assuming that damage equals weakness. Mangiafuoco built it this way. He understood presence.
