# The Cat

> [!example] At a Glance
> The Fox's silent partner — a large, unremarkable cat who feigns clumsiness and says nothing. He is easy to underestimate, and that is precisely the point. Where [[The Fox]] dazzles, the Cat watches. He is the muscle, the lookout, the shadow that moves while you're distracted by the performance.

## Appearance
A broad, grey cat dressed plainly — functional clothes, nothing that draws the eye. He walks with a slight stumble, occasionally knocking into things, which witnesses interpret as ineptitude. Delia the postwoman remembers him vividly: *"bumping into things."* He is almost certainly not bumping into things by accident.

## Personality
The Cat does not speak — or at any rate, has not been heard to. He communicates with [[The Fox]] through looks, small gestures, and the occasional action. He seems to follow orders without question and without evident moral concern. He is not a schemer; he executes. Whether he is capable of greater complexity than this is unclear. He may simply be a man who found, early in life, that silence was the safest performance.

## Background
Little is known of the Cat independently. He appears wherever [[The Fox]] appears, and the two are always described together. In the book's tradition, he feigns blindness; in the film he is mute and slapstick. In Krat, he is quiet and observant, which is more dangerous than either. He ate at the [[The Red Crawfish Inn]] without speaking a word and left without paying.

## Role in the Campaign
The Cat serves as a reliable indicator that [[The Fox]] is operating nearby. Wherever the players encounter the Cat alone, the Fox is not far behind — or has just left. He also functions as the Fox's enforcement when charm fails: physical, practical, and without hesitation.

## Connections
- [[The Fox]] — his partner; the two are never far apart
- [[Pinocchio]] — sat beside him at breakfast; the Cat said nothing and watched everything
- [[The Innkeeper|Mastro Lucone]] — cheated alongside the Fox
- [[Rabbit]] — noted his presence and the side-door exit in his written account
