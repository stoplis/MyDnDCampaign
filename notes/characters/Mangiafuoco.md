# Mangiafuoco

> [!example] At a Glance
> A huge, terrifying puppeteer whose name means "Fire-Eater" — a showman of considerable talent and entirely flexible conscience who travels the region with a puppet theatre that is, depending on one's vantage point, either a marvel or a mechanism of captivity. He has been plastering Krat and the surrounding roads with posters featuring his grinning face. [[Lampwick]] followed those posters. The players will eventually follow the same road.

## Appearance
Enormous — both in height and in presence. He has a beard like a theatrical villain's and the gestures of a man who has spent his life performing to the back row. He wears a coat of many colours, which is either magnificent or grotesque depending on the light. He smells of smoke and sawdust and something theatrical. When angry, which is often, his voice carries across a field.

## Personality
Mangiafuoco is a complicated antagonist. In the original Collodi book, he is fearsome in appearance but secretly soft-hearted — he shows [[Pinocchio]] mercy despite himself. In the film he is Stromboli: darker, greedier, with a cage for every performing puppet and firewood waiting for those who can no longer earn. In Krat, he exists somewhere between these poles. He is a businessman first and an artist second, and the line between performer and prisoner in his theatre has become, over the years, difficult to see. He is not without feeling. He is without restraint.

> [!warning]- GM Notes — The Theatre's Secret
> Mangiafuoco's wagon and theatre are more than they appear. [[Lampwick]] went willingly. But willingness has a shorter shelf life than Mangiafuoco lets on, and the architecture of the theatre — the way it is set up, the contracts the performers sign — makes leaving harder than it looked on the poster. He is not connected to [[The Fox]] and [[The Cat]] directly, but they operate in the same ecosystem of exploitation. Whether he has connections to deeper forces in the campaign (the Alchemists, the Coachman) is a thread the GM can develop.

## Role in the Campaign
Mangiafuoco is the chapter-two-or-three antagonist: the mid-tier villain with a performing face and a predatory interior. He is the reason [[Lampwick]] is missing and the setting for one of the campaign's most emotionally complicated rescues. The players must navigate his theatre, find Lampwick, and decide how to get him out — which involves understanding the difference between a door that's locked and a door that's just always been closed.

## Connections
- [[Lampwick]] — his newest performer; came willingly; does not yet know the cost
- [[Pinocchio]] — the subject of a faked testimonial on his posters; the boy has never been to his theatre
- [[The Schoolmaster]] — noticed the poster obsession weeks ago
- [[Tigger]] — ended up locked in one of his cages, which Mangiafuoco deeply regrets, because the creature will not stop bouncing
- [[Gopher]] — has been tunnelling beneath the theatre and has undermined a load-bearing support column

> [!tip] GM Note — Running Mangiafuoco
> He never says the Coachman's name. Only *"him"* or *"he"* or *"the man."* A **DC 15 Insight** check reveals this is not fear exactly — it is grief held at arm's length by the refusal to name it. Saying the name would make the loss real in a way he cannot survive.
>
> He can be temporarily calmed — he will not stop fighting, but he will speak between attacks — if the players acknowledge that his work was genuinely brilliant. Not flattery: *acknowledgment*. Players who demonstrate they actually looked at the backstage flats, saw the playbills, understood what was built here and then destroyed, will reach something in him. What they get in return are fragments: who came, what was taken, the name he will not say.
>
> He is not a monster. He is a brilliant man eaten by grief and obsession who cannot see the shape of what he has become.
>
> Players who operated the trapdoor levers from the Backstage control board can use a **bonus action** to disrupt his *Master of the Stage* and *Vanish Below* movement — forcing him to find an alternate exit as a full action.
